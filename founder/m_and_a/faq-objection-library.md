# FAQ OBJECTION LIBRARY — 25 Common Buyer Pushbacks

**Usage:** Memorize 5-6 relevant to your situation. Use in calls/emails.

---

## PHASE 1: OUTREACH OBJECTIONS

### ❌ "Why should we buy instead of build?"

**Buyer concern:** We have engineering; we can build this ourselves in 6-12 months.

**Your response (choose one):**

1. **Founder retention angle:**
   > "Build vs. buy isn't really the question—it's about *speed + team*. You'd need 6-12 months + hiring 3-4 engineers. We've already solved the hard problems (product-market fit, enterprise sales, retention). You get our team + code + 200 enterprise customers immediately. That's 12-18 months of work + hiring risk."

2. **Customer angle (if applicable):**
   > "Fair point. But you'd also need to migrate our 200 customers. We have 94% retention, enterprise contracts locked in. Building separately means cannibalizing your own install base or managing two products. Consolidating gets you [X]% revenue uplift in year 1, no migration pain."

3. **Realistic:**
   > "You could build. It'd cost $[2-3M in salaries/overhead]. We're asking $[X]. Faster, lower risk, and you get our existing revenue + team. Your choice."

---

### ❌ "Your growth is slowing—why should we pay full price?"

**Buyer concern:** MoM growth was 15% last quarter; month before was 18%.

**Your response:**

1. **Natural deceleration is normal:**
   > "We're at $[ARR]M ARR. Growth naturally decelerates at this scale. 15% MoM = 435% YoY. We've been profitable for [X] quarters. For a company our size, that growth + profitability is strong."

2. **Inflection point narrative:**
   > "Actually, the slowdown is strategic. We shifted from $[X] ACV to $[Y]k ACV (enterprise deals, longer sales cycles). It looks like deceleration in the short term, but it's higher-quality revenue + better retention. Wait for next quarter's renewal rate and you'll see the play."

---

### ❌ "We want to do a keep-separate model, which lowers the multiple."

**Your response:**

> "I hear that. Keep-separate has advantages for you—no integration risk, standalone valuation. But the downside is you lose the cross-sell upside. Here's what I'd propose: base price reflects keep-separate value. If you hit [X] cross-sell targets in year 1, there's earnout upside for both of us. Deal?"

---

### ❌ "Your churn is too high."

**Buyer concern:** 5% MoM churn seems elevated.

**Your response:**

1. **Normalize for cohort:**
   > "5% MoM churn looks high in isolation. But our 12-month net retention is 110% [or: our logo retention is 92%, revenue retention is 97%]. The monthly number reflects seasonal customer seasonality + one big customer that downgraded. Here's the cohort data..."

2. **Honest but reframing:**
   > "Fair critique. Churn was higher in year 1-2 (product-market discovery phase). Last 6 months, it's stabilized at [X]% with enterprise contracts. And your post-acq playbook will reduce it further."

## PHASE 2: DUE DILIGENCE OBJECTIONS

### ❌ "Your customer concentration is concerning—you have one customer at 18% revenue."

**Your response:**

1. **Risk mitigation narrative:**
   > "Understood. That customer ([Name]) is under contract through [date]. We've already discussed retention with them—they're excited about the combination. Their use case is [specific], and your platform can support [2-3 adjacent use cases] once integrated. I'd frame this as an opportunity, not a risk."

2. **Tier the concentration:**
   > "Here's the breakdown: Top 10 customers = 45% revenue. All are under long-term contracts (avg [X] months remaining). Top customer is [X]% but has [Y] expansion potential. Bottom 50 customers have [X]% churn. We've built diversification into the sales strategy. Give me 30 days, and I'll show you a customer concentration map + risk mitigation plan."

---

### ❌ "Your financial projections seem aggressive."

**Your response:**

> "They're based on [X]. I'm not guessing. Here's the logic: 
> - CAC is $[X] (proven), LTV is $[Y] (12-month cohort data)
> - Pipeline is $[Z] (already named deals, these are real)
> - Conversion rate is [X]% (last 4 quarters averaged)
> 
> I'd rather under-promise on earnout. Let's build conservative projections together—tie them to actual pipeline metrics instead of growth rate assumptions. That way, earnout is achievable."

---

### ❌ "Your tech stack is outdated/different from ours."

**Your response:**

1. **Technical honesty:**
   > "We built with [tech stack] because it solved for [X: scale, speed, cost]. It works. Your stack is [Y]—also good. Post-acquisition, the question isn't 'which is better,' it's 'how do we integrate without migration risk?' I'd suggest we keep both running in parallel for 6 months, then migrate [YourCo] customers to your platform if there's upside. Slower, but safer."

2. **Acknowledge but reframe:**
   > "Fair point. Our tech isn't bleeding-edge, but it's stable and customers love the product. The good news: consolidating onto your platform post-close is a 12-18 month project, not a year-1 crisis. That's a Year 2 investment, not an acquisition blocker."

---

### ❌ "Why should we pay an earnout? Why not just pay in cash now?"

**Your response:**

> "I prefer cash too. But earnout aligns our incentives for [X] months post-close. You want my team heads-down on integration + retention. Earnout ensures we're both motivated. If we hit the targets, I get upside; if we miss, you cap your risk. It's risk-sharing. What earnout structure would feel fair to you?"

## PHASE 3: BINDING & NEGOTIATION OBJECTIONS

### ❌ "We want a [very low] earnout percentage; most deals are [X%]."

**Your response:**

1. **Benchmark transparency:**
   > "I've seen structures from 10%-40% of purchase price, depending on deal type. Keep-separate deals run lower (10-20%); integrated deals run higher (25-40%) because post-acq risk is higher. Our situation is [reason: highly integrated / moderate risk / high synergy]. I'd expect [20-30%]. Here's the logic: [explain your rationale]."

2. **Negotiation lever:**
   > "I hear you want to cap earnout low. If earnout is [X]%, then base price needs to reflect full value. If you want a higher base, earnout is lower. If you want lower earnout, let's make the base price reflect that—but that's a bigger number upfront. Which way do you prefer?"

---

### ❌ "We're pushing for a non-compete of [longer period]."

**Your response:**

> "I understand you want protection. [Longer period] is aggressive for me personally. Standard in this market is [X period] + [X geographic scope]. I'd accept [standard period] + optional non-solicitation of employees for [X months]. Would that work?"

---

### ❌ "We want representation & warranties insurance—you should pay for it."

**Your response:**

> "R&W insurance is standard. The question is who pays and what coverage we buy. If I'm paying, it's lower coverage. If you're paying, you get broader coverage (at your cost). Standard is 'seller pays for standard tier, buyer buys optional upgrade if they want.' Let's find a middle ground: [suggest specific split]."

---

### ❌ "The diligence report showed [problem]—we're cutting price by 20%."

**Your response:**

1. **If genuine issue:**
   > "I understand the concern. Here's what we know: [explain the issue]. It's [manageable / in roadmap / requires X investment]. The financial impact is [Y]—not 20% of company value. Let's price it fairly: either [A] we escrow [X] to cover it, or [B] we agree it's a minor issue and move forward. 20% feels reactive."

2. **If unfounded:**
   > "I respectfully disagree with that conclusion. Here's why: [counter-evidence]. Can we bring [third party: your tech advisor / my advisor] to review? I don't want to fight; I want mutual agreement on facts."

---

### ❌ "We need a [large] employee retention bonus pool—you should sponsor it."

**Your response:**

> "I agree retention is critical. Standard structure: [X]% of purchase price goes to retention bonus pool, vesting over [Y months]. If you want a larger pool, we can discuss—but that comes out of my pocket. I'm happy at [standard]. If you want more, let's adjust the purchase price up and I'll fund the higher pool. What size makes you comfortable?"

## PHASE 4: CLOSING & EARNOUT OBJECTIONS

### ❌ "We're not going to hit the earnout targets."

**Buyer concern** (after deal closes, looking at year 1): Earnout criteria are too aggressive.

**Your response:**

> "I understand. Let's look at the data. [Review Q1/Q2 results]. Here's what we actually need to hit targets: [X specific actions]. I can help move the needle. If genuinely unachievable, let's renegotiate—but let's do it based on data, not intuition."

---

### ❌ "The earnout milestone definitions are vague."

**Your response:**

> "Fair—let's tighten them. 'Revenue' should be defined as [GAAP recognized, invoice date, not cash]. 'Churn' should be [calculated as X across Y period]. 'Customer count' should be [booked logos in your system]. I'll work with your finance team to make it bulletproof."

## 📊 BY-ARCHETYPE EMPHASIS

| Archetype | Top 3 Objections | Best Response Lever |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Strategic** | Build vs buy, Tech stack, Churn | Speed + team, customer retention, consolidation narrative |
| **Private Equity** | Growth feasibility, Margin assumptions, Team retention | Conservative financials, earnout alignment, existing portfolio proof |
| **Consolidator** | Customer concentration, Integration risk, Tech debt | Natural fit, immediate synergies, low disruption |
| **Acqui-hire** | Valuation, Retention, Code quality | Team expertise proof, meaningful role post-acquisition, stability |
| **Crossover** | Valuation, Dilution, Next chapter narrative | Existing relationship, strategic next steps, founder motivation |
