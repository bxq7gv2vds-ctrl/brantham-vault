# Objection Handler Scripts

## Objection #1: "Your valuation is too high"

### Why They Say It
- Standard anchor (lower their first offer)
- Genuine concern about ROI
- Testing your conviction / willingness to move

### Your Response Framework

**Step 1: Acknowledge without conceding**
> "I understand valuation is a key discussion. Let me walk you through the math we've built."

**Step 2: Reframe to value creation**
> "This isn't just what we've done to date — it's what we'll do *together*. Here's how the acquirer typically sees this:"
> - [Show buyer synergy case: cost savings, cross-sell, market expansion]
> - "In our conservative scenario, that's worth €XXX. We're asking for X% of that upside."

**Step 3: Show your walk-away**
> "Our valuation is based on three DCF scenarios. The conservative case alone justifies €XXX. We're not going below that." [Show one-pager]

**Step 4: Pivot to earnout**
> "If you're concerned about valuation, earnout is actually the right mechanic. We earn more only if we *deliver* the synergies we're promising."

### Talking Points
- Comparable transactions in your sector (EV/Revenue, EV/EBITDA)
- Strategic value of revenue (not just EBITDA) — recurring, high-retention customers
- Saved R&D investment (time-to-market advantage)
- Key team IP & relationships (hard to replicate)

### Red Flag
- If buyer doesn't engage with DCF, they may not have done homework → push them to DD process

## Objection #2: "We're worried about customer churn post-acquisition"

### Why They Say It
- Legitimate risk (40-60% of failed M&A have customer/retention issues)
- Testing your confidence in customer loyalty
- May signal they plan changes that could trigger churn

### Your Response Framework

**Step 1: Validate the concern**
> "Retention risk is real in acquisitions. Here's how we've engineered against it:"

**Step 2: Show retention track record**
> "Over the past [X] years, our net retention rate is [X]%. That's because of:
> - [Strong product/service loyalty]
> - [Long contracts with renewal cycles]
> - [Low switching costs barriers actually prevent churn, not the reverse]
> - [Key relationships are with the product, not the founder]"

**Step 3: Retention-as-KPI (earnout linkage)**
> "We should tie earnout KPIs to what you're actually worried about. If we hit 90% customer retention post-deal, we earn the upside."

**Step 4: Integration playbook**
> "Here's our 100-day integration plan. Notice:
> - No immediate price changes
> - Customer success team stays in place
> - Direct customer communication (continuity message)
> - Expanded feature roadmap (value-add)
>
> Churn is lowest when customers *see* more benefit, not less."

### Talking Points
- Customer concentration (if 1-2 customers are >40% revenue, this is real risk)
- Contract lengths (longer = more sticky)
- NPS scores (retention predictor)
- Historical churn rates (model different acquisition scenarios)

### If Buyer Still Worried
> "Let's structure earnout around customer retention. If you're right to worry, the earnout protects you. If I'm right that we won't churn, I earn it. Win-win."

## Objection #3: "Your technology is outdated / needs major investment"

### Why They Say It
- Testing your defensibility (if tech is weak, valuation deflates)
- Genuine tech risk assessment
- May indicate their plan to rebuild (not integrate)

### Your Response Framework

**Step 1: Reframe tech vs. business value**
> "Tech debt ≠ business value destruction. Here's what actually matters:
> - **Customer satisfaction:** [NPS X]. That comes from product-market fit, not latest tech stack.
> - **Revenue stability:** [X% CAGR]. Customers renew because it works, not because it's shiny.
> - **Scalability:** Can we handle [X]x revenue growth with current tech? Yes/No. [Explain]"

**Step 2: Acknowledge real issues (if any)**
> "Yes, we use [tech]. It's been [reliable/scalable/maintained] because:
> - [Specific advantage: cheap to run, developer-friendly, audited]
> - [Not every tech stack needs to be cutting-edge if it's stable]"

**Step 3: Modern stack > legacy tech reality check**
> "If you acquired us and immediately rewrote everything, you'd spend €XXX and 18 months. Meanwhile, you're not adding features. Our approach: incrementally modernize while revenue-generating."

**Step 4: Tie to earnout**
> "Here's what I propose: if post-acquisition you hit [tech migration milestone], no impact on earnout. We're aligned on tech direction."

### Talking Points
- System uptime / reliability stats (if strong)
- Scalability analysis (can handle revenue growth?)
- Regulatory compliance / audit trail (financial, healthcare, data privacy)
- Developer experience (if it's good, it's defensible)

### Red Flag
- If buyer insists on complete rewrite: they may not value your team or customers

## Objection #4: "Key people might leave post-deal"

### Why They Say It
- Most common post-M&A failure reason
- Testing whether you're selling people or company
- Want insurance against brain drain

### Your Response Framework

**Step 1: Separate founder from team**
> "I understand this concern. Let me separate two things:
> - **My role post-close:** [Define clearly: CEO / Advisory / Clean exit]
> - **Core team:** [List 3-5 key people and why they'll *want* to stay]"

**Step 2: Show alignment incentives**
> "Here's how we keep them aligned:
> - **Retention bonuses:** [€XXX total, vested over 18-24m]
> - **Earnout participation:** [They share in earnout if we hit KPIs]
> - **Career growth:** [Specific role/title expansion they get]
> - **Culture fit:** [Show why your team likes this buyer's environment]"

**Step 3: Reference track record**
> "In your last [X] acquisitions, what was your key-person retention rate? [Get the number]. We expect to match or beat that."

**Step 4: Lock them down contractually**
> "Let's draft offer letters NOW (pre-close) so they know what they're signing up for. That eliminates surprises."

### Talking Points
- Specific retention bonus amounts (tied to vesting post-close)
- Earnout structure that includes key employees
- Clear roles/titles post-close
- Reference checks on buyer's post-acquisition culture (do their acquired teams actually stay?)

### Critical Action
- **Do NOT** sell deal as "exit for everyone" — frame as "next growth phase with better resources"

## Objection #5: "Revenue is stalling / declining"

### Why They Say It
- If true, valuation deflates materially
- Testing if you're transparent
- May indicate market headwinds

### Your Response Framework

**Step 1: If UNTRUE — address directly**
> "Actually, looking at [metric], we're at [actual trend]. [Show graph.]
> - Last 3 months: +X% MoM
> - Seasonality: [Explain if relevant]
> - Compare to market: We're growing faster than [competitor], same as [peer]"

**Step 2: If PARTIALLY TRUE — explain the story**
> "We had a tough Q[X] because [one-time event]. Here's why it's now behind us:
> - [Specific customer churn]: Resolved by [action taken]
> - [Market downturn]: Now recovering (see [metric])
> - [Product pivot]: New go-to-market is [X] months in, showing [metrics]"

**Step 3: If TRUE — reframe as opportunity**
> "Yes, Q[X] was challenging. But that's exactly why you're a good acquirer:
> - We've identified root cause: [X]
> - We're 3 months into fix: [metric improving]
> - Your distribution / [buyer advantage] will accelerate that fix
> - Valuation reflects current state, not recovered state"

**Step 4: Use caution on earnout**
> "If there's revenue risk, earnout becomes more valuable to both of us:
> - You don't overpay for uncertain future
> - We get upside if we fix the problem
> - Aligned incentives"

### Talking Points
- Cohort retention (older cohorts retaining better than new ones?)
- Win/loss analysis (why are we losing deals?)
- Market research (is the sector declining, or are we losing share?)
- Competitive positioning (gaining or losing to specific competitors?)

### Red Flag
- If you don't have credible story for decline: accept lower valuation, push earnout harder

## Objection #6: "The market is saturating / won't grow"

### Why They Say It
- Concerns about total addressable market (TAM)
- Testing your growth thesis
- May indicate buyer sees declining opportunity

### Your Response Framework

**Step 1: Provide TAM analysis**
> "Let me share our TAM breakdown:
> - **Total addressable market (SAM):** €XXX
> - **Serviceable obtainable market (SOM):** €XXX (our realistic 5-yr target)
> - **Current share:** X% (room to grow X%)"

**Step 2: Show your growth levers (beyond market growth)**
> "Even if the market is flat, here's our growth:
> - **Market share gains:** Moving from X% to Y% (getting it from [competitors])
> - **Product expansion:** [New features/segments] (expand ACV by X%)
> - **Geo expansion:** [Enter new markets] (€XXX opportunity)
> - **Vertical expansion:** [New customer types] (X% TAM increase)"

**Step 3: Buyer-specific synergies**
> "As part of your company, we get:
> - [Immediate cross-sell]: +[€XXX revenue day-1]
> - [Customer base leverage]: Sell to [buyer's existing customers]
> - [Technology reuse]: Cut costs by [X]%
> - These overcome any market saturation"

**Step 4: Accept smaller TAM, smaller valuation**
> "If you believe TAM is smaller than we do, that's fine. Our valuation reflects our TAM assumption. Let's agree on realistic TAM and price accordingly."

### Talking Points
- Secondary market research (analyst reports, Gartner, etc.)
- Customer expansion potential (ACV growth YoY)
- Competitive dynamics (TAM contracting or market share shifting?)

## Objection #7: "We could build this ourselves cheaper"

### Why They Say It
- Classic negotiation anchor
- Possible genuine assessment ("make vs. buy" question)
- Testing whether you've created defensibility

### Your Response Framework

**Step 1: Acknowledge the math**
> "You're right that *building* something is possible. Let's do the math:
> - **Build timeline:** [12-24 months typical]
> - **Build cost:** [€XXX in eng, PM, QA]
> - **Opportunity cost:** [€XXX in foregone revenue from other priorities]
> - **Total cost-to-parity:** €XXX
> - **Our ask:** €XXX
>
> So build vs. buy is actually closer than it seems."

**Step 2: Show the *real* differences**
> "But it's not just about cost. You'd also get:
> - **Customers:** [X paying customers, €XXX ARR] (you'd have zero)
> - **Team:** [X engineers/PMs who know the product] (requires hiring/ramp)
> - **Production maturity:** Battle-tested in [industry], not beta
> - **Time-to-revenue:** Start collecting revenue day-1, not month 18"

**Step 3: Reframe as strategic cost vs. revenue**
> "Think about it differently:
> - **Option A (Build):** €XXX cost, 18-month delay, 60% success rate
> - **Option B (Buy):** €XXX cost, 1-month close, 95% success rate
>
> For a [€XXX] buyer, the risk premium alone justifies acquisition."

**Step 4: Walk away if needed**
> "If you genuinely believe you can build this better/cheaper, then maybe acquisition isn't the right move. And that's fine — we have our BATNA."

### Talking Points
- Time-to-market reality (technical reality, not optimistic plan)
- Team hiring costs (senior engineers are expensive)
- Customer switching costs (customers stick with proven product)
- Opportunity cost to your company (what else would your team *not* build?)

### Dangerous Response
> "You can't build this" ← False. Everyone can build anything. Focus on cost of delay and risk.

## Objection #8: "Your margins are thin compared to competitors"

### Why They Say It
- Cost structure concern
- Testing your operational efficiency
- Possible renegotiation trigger

### Your Response Framework

**Step 1: Validate the margin data**
> "Let's look at apples-to-apples:
> - Competitor A does [X] at [Y] margins
> - We do [similar/different] at [Z] margins
> - Key difference: [Explain margin gap]"

**Step 2: Show path to margin expansion**
> "Our margin is currently [X]% because of:
> - **R&D intensity:** We're still [growing/building], not optimized
> - **Sales mix:** We have [enterprise/SMB] mix that's [less/more] efficient
> - **Operational scale:** At €XXX ARR we're [pre-optimal, optimal, over-leveraged]
>
> Post-acquisition, here's our margin roadmap:
> - Y1: [X]% (add [operational lever])
> - Y2: [X]% (add [operational lever])
> - Y3: [X]% (reach [peer benchmark])"

**Step 3: Link to earnout**
> "Our path to margin improvement is our earnout. If we hit [margin target], we earn [earnout]. If not, you save that earnout and reinvest in growth instead."

**Step 4: Don't let margin drive down valuation too much**
> "Margin is a *multiple* on profitability, not a discount to valuation. Our revenue growth more than compensates for current margins. Compare us on EV/Revenue, not EBITDA margin."

### Talking Points
- Margin drivers (what % is sales vs. COGS vs. R&D?)
- Operational leverage (how much margin expands if revenue grows 20%?)
- Benchmarks (show peer group margins at your size, not at their size)

## Universal Patterns

### What Works Across All Objections

1. **Acknowledge without conceding** — "That's a fair point" doesn't mean you agree
2. **Reframe to value, not cost** — Shift from "is it expensive" to "what's the ROI"
3. **Show data, not emotion** — Graphs, comps, customer stories beat assertions
4. **Offer alternative structures** — If price is objection, try earnout, seller note, earnout
5. **Reference buyer's strategy** — "This aligns with your [strategic priority]"
6. **Walk away ready** — "If you don't see the value, we're fine going our separate ways"

### Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- ❌ Defensive tone ("That's not true, you're wrong")
- ❌ Over-explaining ("We have 47 reasons why...")
- ❌ Desperate concession ("Okay, we'll drop €XXX")
- ❌ Changing story ("Last week we said Y, now we're saying Z")
- ❌ Attacking buyer ("Your last acquisition failed")

## Pre-Negotiation Prep

For each buyer, prepare:
- [ ] 1-pager on their past [X] acquisitions (retention rates, synergies realized)
- [ ] Their strategic priorities (from investor calls, analyst reports)
- [ ] Likelihood they genuinely believe each objection vs. standard anchor
- [ ] Which objections are real risks vs. which are negotiation theater
- [ ] Your concession strategy (if you give on valuation, what do you gain?)
