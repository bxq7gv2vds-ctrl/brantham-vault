# SDE vs EBITDA — What Buyers Actually Use

## Quick Definition

| Metric | Formula | Who Uses It | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| **SDE** | Net Income + Owner Salary + Owner Discretionary Expenses | PE buyers (small deals), individual acquirers | Reflects real cash owner can extract |
| **EBITDA** | Revenue − COGS − OpEx + D&A adjustments | PE (larger), strategic, public buyers | Normalized for scale, debt capacity, growth investment |

## The Key Difference

**SDE = "What could the new owner take home as salary + benefits?"**
- Adds back: owner salary, benefits, discretionary spend (travel, meals, car, consulting spouse, etc.)
- Removes: non-recurring items (lawsuit settlement, one-time legal fees)
- Used for: Solo operators, service businesses, small SaaS (< $5M ARR typically)

**EBITDA = "What's the normalized operating profit?"**
- Removes: D&A (non-cash), interest (financing choice), taxes (geography choice)
- Keeps: reinvestment capex, growth-related spending
- Used for: Scalable businesses, multi-location, PE/VC-backed

## Example: $10M ARR SaaS

```
Revenue                        $10,000,000
COGS (hosting, payment fees)   $1,500,000
Gross Profit                   $8,500,000

S&M                            $3,000,000
R&D                            $1,500,000
G&A (admin, accounting, legal) $1,200,000
Total OpEx                     $5,700,000

EBITDA                         $2,800,000  (28% margin)
Depreciation & Amort           $200,000
Interest (debt)                $100,000
Taxes (25%)                    $625,000

Net Income                     $1,875,000

---
Founder salary (W-2)           $300,000 (included in G&A above)
Other owner benefits           $100,000 (car, health, meals)
Discretionary owner spend      $150,000 (consulting wife, travel, office perks)

SDE = $1,875,000 + $300,000 + $100,000 + $150,000 = $2,425,000
```

## Multiple Impact

| Buyer Type | Uses | Typical Multiple | Deal Size | Valuation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Individual / PE (small) | SDE | 3–5x | <$5M | $2.4M × 4x = **$9.6M** |
| PE (growth) / Strategic | EBITDA | 6–10x | $5–50M | $2.8M × 8x = **$22.4M** |

**Note**: Same company, **2.3x valuation difference** just based on metric choice.

## When to Push SDE vs EBITDA

### Use SDE When:
- Your owner salary is abnormally high (>$500k/yr) → buyer will replace you
- You have discretionary expenses (car, consulting family) → reclassify as necessary
- Buyer is individual/smaller PE → they think in personal cash extraction
- Goal: maximize proceeds (can be more generous with addbacks)

### Use EBITDA When:
- Your business is truly scalable (recurring revenue, no owner dependency)
- Buyer is PE/strategic with financial benchmarks
- You have real reinvestment needs (salaries, R&D, infrastructure)
- Goal: position for growth (show efficiency, not just current cash)

## Red Flags by Metric

**SDE Red Flag:** Buyer wants to use SDE but you have low owner salary → they're likely to force salary cuts post-close to inflate "true" SDE.

**EBITDA Red Flag:** Buyer includes your salary as an "add-back" to EBITDA → they're undercounting real operating costs.

## Negotiation Tactic

**Your play**: If buyer uses EBITDA, defend your G&A headcount: "That's not discretionary—that's team we need to operate." If buyer uses SDE, push back on discretionary addbacks: "Only include what a new owner will absolutely replicate."

---

**TL;DR:** SDE ≈ cash owner can extract today. EBITDA ≈ normalized operating profit. Same business, different metric, 2–3x valuation swing. Know which your buyer uses, then negotiate the adjustments.
