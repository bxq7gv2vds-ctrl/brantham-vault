# Objection Handler: "Too Much Key Person Risk"

**When you hear it:**
- "If [Founder] leaves, the business falls apart."
- "You don't have a management team. It's just you."
- "What happens if you leave post-acquisition?"

---

## **Bad Response** ❌

"I'm not going anywhere. I'm excited to stay and help scale this."

*Why it fails:* Every founder says this. Buyer heard it from the last 10 founders they acquired, and half left within 18 months. Buyer knows you're self-interested; your word isn't enough.

---

## **Good Response** ✅

**Step 1: Acknowledge the real risk**

"You're right—there's key person risk. Here's what I've built and what's actually dependent on me:

| Function | Dependent on Me? | Backup / Owner | Risk Level |
|----------|---|---|---|
| **Product vision** | 60% | [VP Product] owns day-to-day roadmap | Medium |
| **Customer relationships** | 80% (for top 5) | [Sales lead] has relationships, I'm transferring | High |
| **Engineering decisions** | 20% | [CTO] owns architecture | Low |
| **Financial strategy** | 90% | [CFO] owns operations, I'm documenting | High |
| **Company culture** | 70% | [COO] owns people/ops | Medium |

**Honest takeaway:** If I leave, company still functions, but revenue retention drops [X]%, and [Function A/B] lose momentum."

**Step 2: Show the transition plan**

"To de-risk this, here's what happens post-close:

**Months 1–3 (Honeymoon):**
- I do all customer calls (vs. delegating)
- I document [Function A] decisions (playbooks, decision frameworks)
- [Backup person] shadows every call / decision

**Months 4–12 (Handoff):**
- [Backup person] leads 50% of customer calls; I'm present, they own
- I review, don't decide
- New customers introduced to [Backup person] first
- My role shifts to: strategy + one-off escalations

**Post-Year 1:**
- [Backup person] owns [Function A]
- I'm available for strategic decisions + board meetings

By Year 2, I'm not a bottleneck. You've got a functioning management team."

**Step 3: Prove you've built institutional knowledge**

"I'm not the only person who understands this company:

- **[CTO] built [X]** — They know product depth as well as I do
- **[Sales lead] has [X] customer relationships** — They know customer pain points
- **[Ops/CFO] tracks [X] metrics** — They know financial levers
- **[COO] runs [X]** — They know culture + team

If I died tomorrow, you'd have operations disruption but not company destruction."

**Step 4: Lock it with compensation (earnout/retention bonus)**

"Here's how I'm putting my money where my mouth is:

| Incentive | Amount | Ties to |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| **Cash at close** | [X]% of purchase price | Immediate liquidity |
| **Earnout (12 months)** | [Y]% of purchase price | Revenue/customer retention hits (I stay through measurement) |
| **Equity rollover** | [Z]% of purchase price as equity | Long-term upside if acquisition succeeds (I stay vested) |
| **Retention bonus** | $[A]k | 12-month employment term minimum |

I'm economically incentivized to stay and make this work. If earnout misses, I lose $[Y]k. That's real."

---

## **Script Template**

```
Buyer: "Lot of key person risk here. What if you leave?"

You: "Fair concern. Let me separate what I do vs. what the company needs:

I personally own:
- Tier-1 customer relationships (top 5, $[X]M ARR)
- Financial strategy (board decisions, capital allocation)
- Product vision (long-term roadmap calls)

My team owns:
- [CTO]: Product execution, architecture
- [Sales]: Most customer relationships, renewals
- [Ops]: Hiring, systems, day-to-day operations

If I left tomorrow, what breaks:
- Top customer calls would need transition (1–2 months)
- Strategic decisions slow (probably good for you, actually)
- Culture would be fine (ops team is strong)

But here's what de-risks me leaving:
1. I'm financially incentivized to stay ([Y]% of deal price is earnout)
2. My replacement is already here ([Backup person], trained and ready)
3. You get a management team, not just me

Let's structure the earnout around key metrics 
(revenue retention, customer growth). 
If those hit, I stay; if they miss, we both lose money."
```

## **Contract Language: Key Person Clauses**

Buyer will propose:

| Clause | Your Position |
|--------|---|
| **Key person = you must stay 12 months** | ✅ Acceptable. Document what happens if you get hit by a bus (CEO succession plan) |
| **Key person = you forfeit [X]% earnout if you leave** | ⚠️ Negotiate: capped at [Y] months, not full 12 |
| **Key person = you personally guarantee customer retention** | ❌ Reject. You can't control customer churn alone |
| **Non-compete for [X] years** | ⚠️ Negotiate: [18–24] months reasonable, [5] years is excessive |
| **Change of control bonus (you get paid if you stay 12 months)** | ✅ Ask for this if earnout is at risk |

**Your play:**
- Accept 12-month employment term (standard)
- Ask for earnout tied to business metrics (revenue, NRR), not personal guarantee
- Request succession plan (who replaces you if you leave?)
- Negotiate non-compete scope (don't ban you from your industry, just [specific competitors])

---

## **When This Response Doesn't Work**

If buyer says: "We want [founder] to stay [X] years post-close, or we're not buying."

**Your options:**

1. **Accept founder lockup** (12–24 months reasonable; 3+ years is golden handcuffs)
   - Get paid retention bonus ($[X]/month) in addition to deal price
   - Document job description (you're not taking a demotion)

2. **Push back on timeline**
   - "I'll stay 18 months if you accelerate my equity vesting and pay bonus."
   - Counter: You own the company; you're not going back to being an employee

3. **Propose alternative**
   - "I'll stay as advisor (10h/week) + earn earnout on revenue metrics. But I'm not staying full-time."

4. **Walk the deal**
   - If buyer won't budge, acquisition isn't right for you
   - You built something valuable; other buyers will want it

---

## **Preemptive Strike**

In your pitch deck, have a slide:

"**Management Team + Succession Plan**
- [Founder]: [Role], [X] years in role, personally owns [Functions A/B]
- [CTO]: [Role], [X] years, owns [Functions C/D]
- [Sales]: [Role], [X] years, owns [Functions E/F]
- [COO]: [Role], [X] years, owns [Functions G/H]

If founder leaves, [CTO/Sales/COO] assumes leadership. 
Post-acquisition, we'll hire [CFO/CEO] to manage growth."

This shows competence + maturity. You're not a one-person show.

---

## **Red Flags to Avoid**

🚩 Don't say: "I'll definitely stay." (You don't know the future)
🚩 Don't promise: "I'll work for [X] years." (You might hate working there)
🚩 Don't deflate: "Yeah, I'm kind of burnt out." (Buyer will demand lower price or earnout)
🚩 Don't over-promise: "I'll personally ensure revenue hits [X]." (You can't control buyer's decisions)

---

## **Numbers That De-Risk**

🎯 **CEO/COO in place** (you're not the only operator)
🎯 **Top [X] customers < 60% of revenue** (you don't own all relationships)
🎯 **[Team member] has been here [X] years** (continuity, not new hire)
🎯 **Earnout tied to business metrics, not personal obligations** (objective measurement)
