# 3-Round Negotiation Scripts

Use these verbatim in calls with buyer. Adjust names/numbers only.

---

## ROUND 1: Anchoring + Expectations Setting

**Your goal:** Establish high anchor, signal seriousness, scope the negotiation.

**Buyer's likely opening:** "$X is our offer based on $Y multiple."

**Your response (verbatim):**

> "Thanks for the offer. I appreciate you showing me a specific number. Before I react, I want to make sure we're aligned on what we're valuing.
>
> Our EBITDA is $[X], and we're confident we'll hit $[X+20%] next year given [1–2 concrete reasons: new customer wins, pipeline, etc.].
>
> Market multiples for a business like ours with [recurring revenue / net retention >100% / etc.] are 7–10x EBITDA in the current environment. So a realistic range is $[Y–Z].
>
> Your offer of $[lower] is below that range. I'm not rejecting it outright, but it tells me we're starting negotiation from different anchors.
>
> Are you open to exploring a structure where we find common ground—maybe cash + earnout tied to specific metrics you can audit?"

**Why this works:**
- You don't reject outright (keeps door open)
- You reframe around multiples, not emotions ("I think I'm worth more")
- You signal you'll only move if they move
- You introduce earnout = wiggle room for both

---

## ROUND 2: Realignment + Bundled Moves

**Buyer's likely response:** "$[slightly higher] is the max we can do in cash. Could you do earnout?"

**Your response (verbatim):**

> "I appreciate you moving. Let me be direct: the gap between where we are and where I need to be isn't just price—it's structure.
>
> Here's what would work for me:
>
> - **Cash**: $[0.75× your range] — that's a real compromise from my anchor
> - **Earnout**: 15% of purchase price over 2 years, tied to revenue targets *you can influence*, not marketing spend changes
> - **Founder role**: I'll stay 12 months to ensure smooth transition, locked-in salary + existing equity acceleration clause
>
> This gives you downside protection (you don't pay the earnout unless we hit revenue targets) and me upside (if the business performs as I expect).
>
> Can you take this structure back to your board/team and see if it works?"

**Why this works:**
- Bundled concession (price down, but time + earnout up)
- You own the earnout conditions (revenue, not external factors)
- You give specific role/timeline (removes uncertainty)
- You ask them to move, not you

---

## ROUND 3: Final Close or Walk

**Buyer's response:** Either (A) "We can do $[number] with this structure," or (B) "Still too high."

### **Scenario A: Close Range Identified**

**Your response (verbatim):**

> "I think we're close. Before we move to legal, I want to lock in the details:
>
> - Purchase price of $[X]: $[Y] cash at close, $[Z] earnout if we hit $[revenue target] by [date]
> - Earnout triggers on quarterly revenue audited by [shared accountant]; if we miss by >10%, it's waived
> - I'm CEO until [month], then [role], locked-in at $[salary] + full equity acceleration on change-of-control
> - Non-compete: [geography], [duration], specific to [market/product]
> - Reps & warranties: You'll purchase R&W insurance at closing (standard cost: ~1% of deal)
> 
> Are those terms what we just discussed? If yes, let's have legal write the LOI and we can finalize details there."

**Why this works:**
- You summarize to avoid misunderstanding
- You include non-negotiables (R&W insurance, earnout mech, your role)
- You specify next step (LOI)
- You sound ready to close

---

### **Scenario B: Still Too High**

**Your response (verbatim):**

> "I hear you. Here's my position: I've moved from my anchor significantly. But I'm not willing to go below $[X] in total value, regardless of structure.
>
> That said, I don't want to be irrational. Let me ask: is the gap a number thing, or is there a structural issue I'm missing? For example:
>
> - Are you uncomfortable with the earnout mechanics?
> - Do you need a longer transition from me?
> - Is there a liability concern I should address?
>
> If it's purely the number and we genuinely can't bridge it, I think we need to step back and consider if this deal makes sense for both of us. I'm not walking, but I also won't discount further unless something material changes.
>
> What's your read?"

**Why this works:**
- You hold your line (don't keep dropping price)
- You signal you're rational, not emotional
- You ask clarifying questions (maybe they have real concerns, not just price)
- You imply you can walk (credible walkaway)

**If they still say "too high":**

> "Okay, I respect that. Let's not force this. I'll keep the business running, and if your appetite changes in 6 months when we've hit our targets, we can revisit. Alternatively, if there are other strategic buyers you think would be a better fit, I'm open to hearing them."

This plants a seed: **they might lose the deal if they don't move.**

## Anti-Patterns: Avoid These

| Don't Say | Why | Say Instead |
|---|---|---|
| "I'm insulted by your offer" | Emotional, kills negotiation | "That's below market. Here's why: [data]" |
| "My investor/advisor says the value is $X" | Sounds like you're hiding behind others | "Based on comps and our growth, we're worth $X" |
| "I'll come down to $Y if you move" | Too eager; signals you'll keep dropping | "Here's my final position: $X cash + $Z earnout" |
| "I don't know if I even want to sell" | Confuses the signal (walk is OK, flip-flopping isn't) | "I'm only selling at the right price/terms" |

---

## Timing Notes

- **Round 1 call**: 20–30 min. Set tone, don't close.
- **Round 2 call**: Wait 2–3 business days after Round 1. Gives them time to recalibrate.
- **Round 3 call**: Same day or next day if they've moved. Momentum matters.

**Between rounds**: Communicate only via email (paper trail) unless discussing confidential terms.

---

**Practice these scripts with your lawyer or advisor before the real call. The first time you say this should NOT be with the buyer.**
