# OBJECTIONS HANDLER — PHASE-BASED REBUTTALS

**Purpose:** Anticipate buyer objections by phase. Fast rebuttals that reframe the conversation.  
**Use:** Before each call/email, review 2-3 likely objections for that phase  
**Format:** Objection → Why they say it → Your rebuttal → Follow-up question  

---

## PHASE 1: OUTREACH (Email → First Call)

### Objection 1A: "We're not looking to acquire right now"

**Why they say it:**
- They haven't thought about you yet
- They want to see traction first
- They're being polite (standard response)

**Your rebuttal:**
> "I understand. We're not actively shopping either — but we just hit $XM ARR and wanted to explore if there's strategic fit given your [expansion into category / footprint in our market / product roadmap]. Would a 15-minute conversation hurt to explore?"

**Follow-up:**
- "Who on your team should I loop in on future updates?" (Get them invested)
- "When would be a good time to check in if things evolve?" (Calendar reminder)

---

### Objection 1B: "Send us a teaser and we'll review"

**Why they say it:**
- Lazy buyer. Wants to filter you themselves.
- They're genuinely interested but busy.
- Red flag: If they never follow up.

**Your rebuttal:**
> "Happy to — but I've found teaser-only review often misses the strategic angles. Could we do a quick 15-min call first so I can frame what matters most for your team? Then I'll send you a crisp one-pager after."

**Follow-up:**
- Book the call immediately (don't wait for teaser review)
- If they refuse: Lower priority, move on

---

### Objection 1C: "We acquired something similar last year"

**Why they say it:**
- They don't know about your product (assume competitor confusion)
- They literally did acquire a competitor (check Crunchbase)
- They're signaling "not interested"

**Your rebuttal:**
> "Ah, I think there might be confusion. You likely know [competitor name] — we're actually the opposite. We focus on [differentiation]. Their strength is X, ours is Y. Given you just acquired them, you probably have the budget — would make sense to chat about how we'd complement the strategy?"

**Follow-up:**
- Don't push if they acquired a direct competitor (likely acqui-hire or shut-down strategy)
- Pivot to: "Who else on your team should we meet for future integration conversations?"

---

## PHASE 2: INITIAL CONVERSATIONS (First Call → LOI)

### Objection 2A: "Your valuation is too high"

**Why they say it:**
- They always say this (anchoring tactic)
- They have lower multiples in mind than reality
- You haven't proven growth trajectory yet

**Your rebuttal — Tactic 1 (If valuation is defensible):**
> "I hear you. Most buyers in SaaS are pricing at 8-10x ARR for companies like ours with 50%+ growth. We're at [X]x, which is actually below median for our profile. Let me send you our financial normalization + comps — happy to walk through where the multiple comes from."

**Your rebuttal — Tactic 2 (If you have BATNA):**
> "Understood. Our walk-away price is $XM based on [internal growth projections / investor feedback / strategic value]. If that's not in your range, we might not be the right fit. But if you see strategic value, let's talk about how to structure this."

**Follow-up:**
- Email: financial normalization + DCF model
- Ask: "What multiple range were you thinking?" (Get their number)
- Counter: Use comps + your metrics to justify

---

### Objection 2B: "We'd need to integrate your product into ours"

**Why they say it:**
- They don't understand standalone value
- They're planning shut-down or tuck-in (red flag)
- They haven't thought about customer communication yet

**Your rebuttal:**
> "Makes sense. We've modeled a few integration paths. The best scenario for earnout is [standalone growth / customer retention]. Would it make sense to keep [your product] running independently for Year 1-2, then integrate based on what the data shows? That de-risks the acquisition and lets us hit earnout targets."

**Follow-up:**
- Ask directly: "Walk me through your 100-day integration plan for our product"
- Red flag if answer is vague

---

### Objection 2C: "We need to do full due diligence before offering"

**Why they say it:**
- Legitimate. They always want DD first.
- They're not serious (stalling tactic).
- You haven't given them enough to bite.

**Your rebuttal:**
> "Totally understand. We've actually prepped a data room with our last 3 years of financials, customer contracts, tech stack docs. We can start DD immediately — but it'd help to align on a rough valuation range first so we're both operating in the same ballpark. Would a preliminary LOI help frame the DD scope?"

**Follow-up:**
- Don't do full DD without at least a term sheet outline
- Risk: You spend 3 weeks on DD, they lowball at the end

---

## PHASE 3: LOI & DEAL STRUCTURE (LOI → SPA)

### Objection 3A: "We want 50%+ of the deal as earnout"

**Why they say it:**
- They're risk-averse (want you to earn your keep)
- They're cash-constrained (can't pay it all now)
- They're planning to deprioritize your product (red flag)

**Your rebuttal — If cash-constrained:**
> "I get the risk concern, but 50%+ earnout shifts the risk entirely to us. You control the post-close strategy, budget, and hires — which means you control whether we hit earnout. Counter: 30% earnout, 1-year term, standalone metrics that we both agree on now. We both have skin in the game but you have operational control."

**Your rebuttal — If they're just negotiating:**
> "50% creates misaligned incentives. If we're both focused on growth, 20-30% earnout is standard. If you think we won't hit targets post-close, maybe we shouldn't do this deal. What are you really concerned about?"

**Follow-up:**
- Demand specific earnout metrics (ARR, NDR, churn) in writing
- "If we hit 100% of targets, you pay 100% of earnout? Or is there a cap?" (Catch their bullshit)

---

### Objection 3B: "We want reps & warranties for 18+ months"

**Why they say it:**
- Standard buyer tactic (hedge against undisclosed liabilities)
- They're paranoid about tech debt / IP issues
- It's negotiable

**Your rebuttal:**
> "Most warranties are 12-18 months, but the long tail (environmental, IP) extends 3-5. What specific reps are you most concerned about? Let's make those rock-solid with indemnification insurance instead of keeping you in escrow for 2 years. Escrow should be 10-15%, not 25%."

**Follow-up:**
- Negotiate: General reps = 18mo, IP reps = 3yr, tax = 5yr
- Get reps & warranties insurance (shifts liability from you to insurer)

---

### Objection 3C: "Your customer concentration is too high"

**Why they say it:**
- Real risk. Top 3 customers = 60%+ ARR is red flag
- Churn post-acquisition is their concern
- It affects earnout targets

**Your rebuttal:**
> "Fair point. But notice churn has been <2% YoY — we have long contracts + strong NPS. I've got retention letters from top 3 customers confirming their commitment. Plus, we can structure earnout with a [customer concentration gate]: if any top customer churns, earnout adjusts. Both of us are aligned on keeping them."

**Follow-up:**
- Provide: Customer retention letters (pre-signed by CFO)
- Demand: Earnout adjustment clause if customer leaves post-close

---

## PHASE 4: DUE DILIGENCE (SPA Drafting → Close)

### Objection 4A: "Our lawyers found IP issues — we need you to indemnify"

**Why they say it:**
- Real or perceived: Open-source dependencies, prior invention, etc
- They want you to carry risk
- It's negotiable via insurance

**Your rebuttal:**
> "What's the specific concern? [Listen.] We can address that in two ways: (1) We indemnify with reps & warranties insurance backing (you're covered), or (2) Escrow holdback covers known issues + insurance covers unknown. Let's bring in counsel to scope the real risk — it's usually smaller than initial DD concern."

**Follow-up:**
- Get your lawyer to negotiate reps & warranties insurance
- Don't personally indemnify (cap at escrow amount)

---

### Objection 4B: "We need key employees to stay 18 months (vesting/lockup)"

**Why they say it:**
- Risk mitigation. They want continuity.
- You might be planning to leave immediately.
- It's standard but negotiable.

**Your rebuttal:**
> "We can commit to [specific people] staying through Year 1. But 18-month lockup with zero flexibility doesn't work for us — life happens. Counter: 12-month core team retention, with [retention bonus] if they stay. Plus acceleration clause if earnout milestones hit early."

**Follow-up:**
- Negotiate: Core team (non-negotiable) vs. optional team (can leave)
- Build retention bonus separate from earnout (incentivizes both)

---

### Objection 4C: "We want to close in 30 days (ASAP)"

**Why they say it:**
- Time pressure (urgency bias)
- Their budget year-end is approaching
- It's a red flag if UNREASONABLE (you need legal review + DD)

**Your rebuttal:**
> "We want to close too, but 30 days is tight for proper legal review + DD. We can compress the timeline if you can commit to: (1) SPA draft within 5 days, (2) DD questions in batches, (3) no major surprises at the table. Fair?"

**Follow-up:**
- 60 days is realistic. 30 days = rushed, mistakes, regrets later.
- Use timeline pressure to extract concessions on other terms

---

## PHASE 5: FINAL NEGOTIATION (SPA Redline → Closing)

### Objection 5A: "The escrow is locked for 18 months — you can't access it"

**Why they say it:**
- They want maximum protection
- Escrow is used to cover indemnification claims
- You need liquidity

**Your rebuttal:**
> "Standard escrow terms are 12 months with release of 50% at month 12, rest at month 18 (minus claims). We need some access in Year 1 for integration costs. Counter: 10-12 month release schedule, claims process caps at 20% of escrow (not unlimited)."

**Follow-up:**
- Negotiate: Escrow should be 10-15%, not 25%
- Release schedule: 1/3 at 6mo, 1/3 at 12mo, 1/3 at 18mo
- Claims cap: Maximum 20% of escrow, not unlimited

---

### Objection 5B: "We're reducing the offer by 10% due to new market data"

**Why they say it:**
- Classic last-minute negotiation
- They're testing if you'll fold
- It's rarely justified

**Your rebuttal:**
> "That's a $X reduction after we've committed 60 days to DD and legal. If something materially changed your view, let's talk through the specific issue. But blanket discounts after LOI is signed aren't how this works. We either stick to terms or we walk."

**Follow-up:**
- Don't accept last-minute price cuts
- Use your BATNA: "Our walk-away is $Y. If you're below that, we'll pass."
- Walk if they're negotiating in bad faith

## TOOLBOX: REBUTTAL TEMPLATES

**For "Too expensive":**
> "I understand cost is a factor. But this is an investment in [growth / risk reduction / strategic fit]. Buying cheap often costs more in integration problems. Let's align on value, then price follows."

**For "We're still exploring options":**
> "Makes sense. We're exploring too, but we'd like to move decisively with the right partner. If you want to stay in process, we need exclusivity after LOI. Otherwise we'll take offers from others."

**For "We need to sleep on it":**
> "Totally fair. Take the time you need. Let's schedule a follow-up call [day/time]. Before then, any questions I can clarify?"

**For "You're not a fit culturally":**
> "Help me understand — what's the mismatch? [Listen.] That's valuable feedback. Here's how we see alignment [specific example]. Can we explore more, or is this a dealbreaker?"

## HOW TO USE THIS

1. **Print by phase** (you're in Phase 2? Print Phase 2-3)
2. **Prep before calls** (review 2-3 likely objections)
3. **Don't memorize** (use as talking points, not scripts)
4. **Customize** (insert your actual numbers, metrics, culture)
5. **Share with advisor** (lawyer + M&A advisor should weigh in on rebuttal)

## RED FLAG OBJECTIONS (Walk if you hear these)

| Objection | Meaning | Action |
|---|---|---|
| "We're just killing the product" | Acqui-hire only | ❌ Walk (unless you want to work there) |
| "We won't make earnout available" | They control metrics | ❌ Walk |
| "We can't do reps & warranties insurance" | They're cheap/shady | ⚠️ Proceed cautiously |
| "You have to take a significant haircut" | Bait-and-switch | ❌ Walk |
