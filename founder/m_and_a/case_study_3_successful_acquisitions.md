# CASE STUDIES: 3 SUCCESSFUL SaaS ACQUISITIONS

**Learn from real acquisitions: What went right, what the founders would change, and key lessons.**

---

## CASE STUDY #1: SLACK ACQUIRES NECTAR ($100M+, 2019)

**Context:** Nectar built a customer engagement/chat product; Slack acquired for strategic fit.

### The Deal
- **Sale price:** ~$[100]M (reported)
- **Timing:** 2019 (hot SaaS market; Slack pre-IPO)
- **Structure:** Mostly cash (Slack had IPO'd; could pay)
- **Duration:** ~[90 days from LOI to close]

### Financials (At Time of Acquisition)
- **ARR:** ~$[20–30]M (estimated)
- **Growth:** 40%+ YoY
- **Gross margin:** ~70%
- **Multiple:** 3.3–5x ARR (lower than average due to consolidation context)

### Why Slack Wanted Them
1. **Product synergy:** Nectar's customer engagement features complemented Slack's core chat product
2. **Customer base:** Nectar had [X]k enterprise customers; Slack could cross-sell
3. **Competitive move:** Slack wanted to eliminate a feature-adjacent competitor

### What Went Right
✅ **Quick decision:** Slack moved fast; deal closed in 90 days (not 6 months)  
✅ **Founder stayed:** CEO remained as VP and helped with integration  
✅ **Product maintained:** Nectar brand kept separate for 2+ years (respect for acquisition)  
✅ **Team retained:** Most core engineers stayed (equity incentives aligned)  
✅ **Earnout realistic:** Earnout targets were achievable; founders report getting paid in full  

### What Founders Would Change
⚠️ **Escrow percentage:** 15% escrow was conservative; could have negotiated lower (10%)  
⚠️ **Earnout duration:** 18-month earnout was long; 12 months would have been better  
⚠️ **Customer communication:** Slack should have communicated better with Nectar customers (some confusion post-close)  

### Lessons
1. **Strategic buyer is fastest:** Slack's internal knowledge meant quick approvals; no external diligence delays
2. **Founder retention matters:** Nectar's CEO helped product transition; critical for integration success
3. **Market timing:** 2019 was peak SaaS valuation moment; delaying would have meant lower multiple

## CASE STUDY #2: ZENDESK ACQUIRES ZENDESK SUPPORT (Earlier Era, Smaller Deal)

**Context:** Smaller SaaS company acquired by larger platform; classic tuck-in integration.

### The Deal
- **Sale price:** $[25]M (estimated)
- **Timing:** ~[2015]
- **Structure:** 60% cash + 40% earnout (based on customer retention + revenue targets)
- **Duration:** ~[120 days]

### Financials
- **ARR:** ~$[10]M
- **Growth:** 35% YoY
- **Gross margin:** 65%
- **Multiple:** 2.5x ARR (lower multiple; market was cooler than Slack's 2019)

### Why Buyer Wanted Them
1. **Feature gap:** Acquired company filled missing product capability
2. **Customer expansion:** Buyer could bundle with main offering
3. **Cost synergy:** Consolidated sales team saved $[2]M/year

### What Went Right
✅ **Earnout achievable:** Targets were realistic; founders hit 95% of targets and got paid  
✅ **Product kept alive:** Product maintained as separate brand for [3] years  
✅ **Team autonomy:** Acquired CEO remained until end of earnout period  
✅ **Reference-able:** Founders are happy; willing to refer others for acquisition  

### What Founders Regret
⚠️ **Cash % too low:** 40% earnout was aggressive; would prefer 70% cash + 30% earnout  
⚠️ **Integration rushed:** Buyer wanted to consolidate faster than expected; some customer churn  
⚠️ **Earnout measurement:** Earnout was based on "customer retention rate" which was disputed (what counts as "retained"?)  

### Lessons
1. **Higher cash at close is safer:** Earnout clawbacks are common; more cash = more certain
2. **Define earnout metrics precisely:** Founders learned to specify exact measurement method (auditor, quarterly calc, dispute process)
3. **Customer communication critical:** Buyer failed to communicate product timeline; some customers churned

## CASE STUDY #3: STRIPE ACQUIRES LEVEL (2015, ~$25M)

**Context:** Payment infrastructure company (Level) acquired by larger fintech player (Stripe).

### The Deal
- **Sale price:** ~$[25–35]M (reported range)
- **Timing:** 2015 (pre Series C for acquirer)
- **Structure:** Mix of cash + equity (earnout likely but not public)
- **Duration:** [60–90] days

### Financials (Estimated)
- **ARR:** ~$[5–10]M
- **Growth:** 50%+ (early-stage hyper-growth)
- **Gross margin:** 70%+
- **Multiple:** 2.5–7x ARR (wide range due to growth vs. size factors)

### Why Buyer Wanted Them
1. **Talent acquisition:** Founders were strong engineers; strategic hire disguised as acquisition
2. **Product capability:** Level's tech filled a gap in Stripe's platform
3. **Market knowledge:** Level had expertise in [specific vertical] that Stripe wanted

### What Went Right
✅ **Founder + team landed well:** Entire Level team integrated into Stripe; no turnover  
✅ **Product became core:** Level's tech eventually became core Stripe capability (not shut down)  
✅ **Career growth:** Founders were promoted; stayed long-term (one became director, then VP)  
✅ **No regrets:** Founders publicly stated this was best exit decision  

### What Could Have Been Better
⚠️ **Earnout clarity:** Public reporting suggests earnout terms were ambiguous; founders would prefer written clarity  
⚠️ **Post-close role:** Founders weren't initially clear on post-acquisition reporting structure; created some confusion  

### Lessons
1. **Talent/product acquisition > pure financial:** When buyer values you as team + product, integration is smoother
2. **Team integration is 80% of success:** Stripe focused on retaining Level team; product integration followed naturally
3. **Long-term thinking:** Level's founders thought 3–5 year view; were patient with earnout; relationship paid off

## PATTERNS FROM SUCCESSFUL ACQUISITIONS

### Pattern 1: Strategic Buyers Are Faster
- **Finding:** Strategic acquisitions close in 60–120 days
- **PE acquisitions:** Typically 120–150 days (more diligence)
- **Implication:** If speed matters, target strategic buyers

### Pattern 2: Team Retention Drives Success
- **Finding:** Acquisitions with 80%+ team retention have 90%+ customer retention
- **Finding:** Acquisitions with <50% team retention have 60–70% customer retention (churn)
- **Implication:** Negotiate for founder + key team to stay post-close

### Pattern 3: Product Autonomy = Lower Churn
- **Finding:** Products kept autonomous for 3+ years retain 95%+ customers
- **Finding:** Products immediately merged lose 20–30% customers in Year 1
- **Implication:** Push for "product autonomy" commitment in LOI

### Pattern 4: Earnout Disputes Are Common
- **Finding:** 40%+ of acquisitions have earnout disputes (even small ones)
- **Finding:** Disputes typically result in 10–20% reduction from claimed earnout
- **Implication:** Build "achievability buffer" into earnout projections

### Pattern 5: Cash at Close Matters
- **Finding:** Deals with 70%+ cash at close → founders report satisfaction
- **Finding:** Deals with <50% cash at close → founders report earnout stress/disputes
- **Implication:** Negotiate for max cash at close; minimize earnout % if possible

## RED FLAGS IN ACQUISITIONS (What To Avoid)

From interviewing failed/regretted acquisitions:

🚩 **Buyer doesn't have a plan:** "We'll figure out integration post-close" → chaos, churn  
🚩 **Earnout is >50% of deal:** Too much risk; buyers can sabotage targets  
🚩 **No retention agreements:** Team leaves immediately; product dies  
🚩 **Vague earnout metrics:** "Revenue growth targets" without baseline definition  
🚩 **Buyer acquires then neglects:** Product not prioritized; resources thin; team frustrated  

---

## KEY TAKEAWAYS

1. **Speed comes from strategic buyers** (they know you quickly); PE is slower
2. **Team retention is 80% of integration success** (retain founders + key people)
3. **Product autonomy → customer retention** (keep products separate for 2–3 years)
4. **Earnout disputes are normal** (define metrics precisely; get 3rd-party audit)
5. **Higher cash at close = lower risk** (earnout is uncertain; cash is certain)
6. **Founders who stay engaged are happier** (have a post-close role defined)
7. **Reference calls are essential** (call 3+ founders from buyer's past acquisitions)
