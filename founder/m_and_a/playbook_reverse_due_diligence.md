# PLAYBOOK: REVERSE DUE DILIGENCE — AUDIT THE BUYER

**You're about to hand over your company. Make sure the buyer is capable, well-intentioned, and able to deliver on promises. Conduct reverse due diligence.**

---

## PRINCIPLE: INFORMATION ASYMMETRY

**Normally, buyers dig deep into sellers.** You should dig equally deep into buyers.

**Key questions:**
- Will they actually pay the earnout?
- Will they maintain your product or kill it?
- Will they retain your team or fire them?
- Do they have integration experience?
- Are they financially sound (can they pay you at close)?

---

## SECTION A: FINANCIAL CAPABILITY (Can They Pay?)

### Questions to Ask:

1. **"Can you walk me through your M&A funding?"**
   - Do they have cash on balance sheet? Line of credit? PE backing?
   - If PE-backed: what's the fund's pedigree? Any issues with prior funds?
   - If debt-financed: what are the debt covenants? Could debt restrict their ability to pay earnout?

2. **"Has your acquisitions ever been delayed or fallen through due to financing?"**
   - If yes: why, and what changed?
   - This reveals whether they've had "surprises" in M&A processes

3. **"Show me your last 3 acquisitions. How much cash was paid at close vs. earnout?"**
   - Ideal: 60–70% cash at close, 30–40% earnout
   - If <50% cash: risky; earnout will likely be fought/clawed back
   - If 100% cash: great for you; means they have capital

4. **"What's your bank account situation? Can you fund a $[X]M deal by [close date]?"**
   - Request: Bank statement or LOI from their lender confirming availability
   - Red flag: Vague answer or "we're working on it"

---

## SECTION B: ACQUISITION TRACK RECORD (Do They Know How?)

### Questions to Ask:

5. **"How many acquisitions has your company done? What's the size range?"**
   - Ideal: 5–10+ acquisitions; demonstrates experience
   - Red flag: First acquisition; inexperienced; likely to make mistakes

6. **"Walk me through your last 3 acquisitions. What went right? What went wrong?"**
   - This reveals their honesty and self-awareness
   - Healthy answer: "We overpaid on Acq #1, learned our lesson, now we're disciplined"
   - Bad answer: "Everything went perfectly" (no one is that good)

7. **"Which of your past acquisitions would you say succeeded? Which did you regret?"**
   - Probe: What made the successful ones succeed?
   - Probe: What happened with the regretted ones? Were founders/teams happy?

8. **"Did you retain the founding team post-close? If not, why?"**
   - Ideal: Yes, we kept most of them (unless they left voluntarily)
   - Red flag: We terminated the entire team (indicates harsh integration)

9. **"Of your past acquisitions, how many are still operating as separate products?"**
   - Ideal: Several; indicates respect for acquired teams/products
   - Red flag: All merged/shutdown; indicates they're just buying customers/tech, not scaling businesses

10. **"How long does your integration process typically take? What's the plan for [my product]?"**
    - Probe: Will my product stay standalone or be merged?
    - Probe: Will my team have autonomy or be absorbed into their org?

---

## SECTION C: POST-ACQUISITION INTEGRATION (What Happens to Your Product?)

### Questions to Ask:

11. **"Walk me through your post-acquisition integration playbook. What are the first 100 days like?"**
    - Red flag: No playbook ("we'll figure it out")
    - Ideal: Detailed 100-day plan with clear milestones

12. **"Will my product stay on its own roadmap or be merged into yours?"**
    - Answer determines: Do you keep autonomy or do you get absorbed?
    - Probe: If merged, what features get prioritized?

13. **"What's your track record on customer retention post-acquisition?"**
    - Ask: Out of your last 5 acquisitions, what % of customers are still with you 24 months later?
    - Ideal: >95% retention
    - Red flag: <80% retention (signals failed integration; churned customers)

14. **"Who will run [my product] after acquisition?"**
    - Best: Existing CEO/founder (autonomy; they know product)
    - OK: Experienced VP (capable; but less autonomy)
    - Worst: Random director ("we'll figure out reporting structure later")

15. **"Will you commit to keeping my product alive for at least [X] years?"**
    - You want: Written commitment in contract (e.g., "maintain product as separate offering for 3 years minimum")
    - Red flag: They won't commit; suggests possible shutdown risk

---

## SECTION D: EARNOUT TRACK RECORD (Will They Pay It?)

### Questions to Ask:

16. **"How much earnout have you paid out in the past? Have you ever clawed back or disputed earnout?"**
    - This is THE critical question
    - Ideal: "We've paid 95%+ of earnout targets; disputes are rare"
    - Red flag: "We've had some disputes" or "earnout targets were ambitious"

17. **"Can you share anonymized examples of earnout calculations and disputes?"**
    - Ask to see: How they measured earnout; how they handled disputes
    - Probe: Were founders satisfied with the outcome?

18. **"Reference check: Can I speak with a founder from [your last 3 acquisitions]?"**
    - THIS is the most valuable due diligence
    - Call them and ask:
      - Did you hit the earnout target? Did you get paid?
      - Would you do it again?
      - Any surprises post-close?
      - Any earnout disputes?

19. **"What happens if earnout targets are missed? Do you adjust the metric or just not pay?"**
    - Red flag: "We just don't pay" (suggests they look for excuses)
    - Ideal: "We're reasonable; if market conditions change, we adjust metric, not the spirit of the deal"

20. **"Will earnout payments be made on schedule (quarterly/annually) or bundled at the end?"**
    - Ideal: Quarterly; gives you cash flow sooner
    - Risky: Annual; you wait 12+ months
    - Worst: One lump sum at end of earnout period; you have no recourse if payment withheld

---

## SECTION E: CULTURE & FOUNDER HAPPINESS (Will Your Team Be Happy?)

### Questions to Ask:

21. **"What's your philosophy on keeping acquired founders in their roles?"**
    - Ideal: "We keep founders CEO; they run the business autonomously"
    - Concerning: "Founders typically transition to board roles; our ops team runs it"

22. **"What % of acquired founders leave within 12 months of closing?"**
    - Ideal: <20% (most stay)
    - Concerning: >50% (suggests unhappy founders)

23. **"How do you handle founders/teams who want to leave post-close?"**
    - Red flag: "We have golden handcuffs; they can't leave" (punitive; breeds resentment)
    - Ideal: "We want them to stay; we offer equity upside and autonomy; they leave if they want"

24. **"Will my team get refreshed equity grants? What's the vesting schedule?"**
    - Ideal: New grants with 4-year cliff, 1-year vesting
    - Red flag: No new grants; old equity becomes worthless post-close

25. **"What's your employee retention bonus strategy? Will you pay our team to stay?"**
    - Ideal: "Yes, we'll offer [X]M retention pool for key people"
    - Risky: "We'll pay if they meet performance targets" (conditional; creates anxiety)

---

## SECTION F: STRATEGIC ALIGNMENT (Do You Share A Vision?)

### Questions to Ask:

26. **"Why do you want to acquire us? What's the strategic thesis?"**
    - Good answers:
      - "You fill a gap in our product roadmap"
      - "Your customer base is our TAM; we can expand together"
      - "You have a technology we need"
    - Bad answers:
      - "Vague synergies; we'll figure it out"
      - "We're just acquiring customers" (product will be killed)

27. **"What would cause you to shut down or sunset my product post-close?"**
    - Probe: Is there a sunset clause in the deal?
    - Ideal: "We'd only sunset if customers migrate to our product organically and choose to"
    - Red flag: "If our product can replicate your features, we'd shut you down"

28. **"How will you market my product post-acquisition?"**
    - Will they maintain a separate brand or fold it into theirs?
    - Will they allocate sales/marketing budget?
    - Or does the product become an "add-on" to their main offering?

---

## SECTION G: RED FLAGS — WALK AWAY IF YOU SEE:

🚩 **Financial red flags:**
- Can't clearly explain how they're funding the deal
- Have had financing fall through on past acquisitions
- Bank won't commit to a letter of intent confirming funds available
- Low cash at close (willing to pay <40% at close)

🚩 **Acquisition track record red flags:**
- This is their first acquisition (inexperienced; will likely stumble)
- Multiple acquisitions have failed or been shutdown
- Founders from past acquisitions are unhappy (call references; they'll tell you)
- Can't articulate lessons learned from acquisitions

🚩 **Post-close integration red flags:**
- No playbook or plan for first 100 days
- Won't commit to keeping your product alive
- Unwilling to keep your team in leadership roles
- History of 50%+ founder departure post-close

🚩 **Earnout red flags:**
- Won't discuss earnout track record (suggests poor performance)
- References say they "disputed" earnout payments
- Earnout conditions are vague (no third-party audit process)
- Earnout clawed back easily (for minor breaches)

🚩 **Cultural red flags:**
- Can't name any happy founders from past acquisitions
- Treat you as a "vendor" rather than a strategic partner
- Pressuring you to "just say yes" without time to decide
- Dismissive of your questions ("we'll handle integration; trust us")

## DUE DILIGENCE INTERVIEW PROCESS

### Step 1: Request Background Documents (Week 1)
1. List of past 5 acquisitions (name, size, date, acquirer)
2. M&A playbook or integration guide (if public)
3. Org chart (to see how acquired companies are structured)
4. Annual report or investor presentation (financials, track record)

### Step 2: Interview 3 Founders from Past Acquisitions (Week 1–2)

**Process:**
- Ask buyer to provide 3 founder references (without filtering)
- Call them directly (not buyer-mediated)
- Ask: "Would you do this acquisition again? Any regrets?"
- Ask: "Did you hit earnout target? Get paid on schedule?"
- Ask: "Was the integration as expected? Any surprises?"

**Interpretation:**
- If all 3 say "Yes, happy, got paid" → Good sign
- If even 1 says "Unhappy, didn't get paid earnout" → Yellow flag; dig deeper
- If all 3 say "No, wouldn't do it again" → Red flag; walk away

### Step 3: Deep-Dive Meeting with Buyer's CEO/CFO (Week 2)

Walk through the 28 questions above. Take notes. Listen for:
- Confidence (they should answer directly)
- Honesty (they should admit past mistakes)
- Clarity (they should have integrated playbooks)

### Step 4: Legal Review

Have your attorney review:
- Buyer's financial statements (are they solvent?)
- Any litigation against the buyer (are they in legal trouble?)
- Debt covenants (can debt restrictions prevent them from paying you?)

---

## REFERENCE CALL SCRIPT

**When you call a founder from buyer's past acquisition:**

> "Hi [Founder], I'm considering being acquired by [Buyer Company]. I see you were acquired by them in [year]. I'd love to get your candid feedback on a few questions:
>
> 1. Walk me through your experience post-acquisition. What went well? What didn't?
> 2. Did you hit your earnout targets? Were payments made on schedule?
> 3. Were there any earnout disputes or clawbacks?
> 4. Would you do the acquisition again if you could go back?
> 5. Any advice for me as you think about their offer?"

**What to listen for:**
- Founder tone (enthusiastic vs. regretful)
- Specific examples (good sign if they can cite specifics)
- Criticisms (good sign if they're candid about problems)
- Would do again (ultimate indicator of satisfaction)

---

## FINAL CHECKLIST: GO/NO-GO DECISION

Before you accept an offer, answer YES to **at least 8 of 10:**

- [ ] Buyer has clear, documented M&A acquisition history (5+ acquisitions)
- [ ] References from past acquisitions are positive (spoke with 2–3 founders; all happy)
- [ ] Buyer can explain how they're funding the deal (bank LOI confirms cash available)
- [ ] Buyer paying >50% cash at close (risk mitigation for you)
- [ ] Buyer has integration playbook (100-day plan documented)
- [ ] Buyer committing to keeping your product alive (written commitment)
- [ ] Earnout track record is good (paid 90%+ of prior earnouts)
- [ ] Earnout disputes are rare (references say earnout paid as agreed)
- [ ] Your founding team will stay (equity grants, retention bonuses offered)
- [ ] Strategic alignment is clear (buyer can articulate why acquiring you)

**Scoring:**
- **8–10 YES:** Proceed. This buyer is trustworthy and capable.
- **6–7 YES:** Proceed with caution. Negotiate stronger terms to offset risk.
- **<6 YES:** Walk away. Too many red flags. Find a better buyer.
