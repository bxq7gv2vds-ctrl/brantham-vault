# Top 10 M&A Mistakes Founders Make

---

## 1. Negotiating Without an Advisor

**Mistake:** Going alone against a buyer's legal team (they have 10 lawyers; you have a spreadsheet).

**Reality:** M&A attorneys cost $5-15k; mistakes cost $500k–$5M.

**Example:** You agree to "reps cap at 2%" without knowing that's negotiable. Market standard is 0.5-1%. **Cost: $100k+**

**Fix:**
- Hire M&A attorney ($15-30k flat fee or hourly)
- Hire advisor (investment banker $50-100k or success fee ~2-3%)
- Before first call, get advisor's blessing on valuation framework

---

## 2. Accepting First Offer Without Countering

**Mistake:** Buyer: "$5M all-cash." You: "Deal!" (You didn't ask if earnout could reduce cash requirement to $4M, with $2M earnout = $6M total)

**Reality:** 80% of first offers have 20-30% room for negotiation.

**Example:** Your $2M ARR company is offered $8M. Market is $10-12M (5-6x). **Cost: $2-4M**

**Fix:**
- Always counter (even if it's +10%)
- Counter with **different structure** (less earnout, more cash)
- Show comps to justify your ask
- Let them talk first; don't anchor too low

---

## 3. Ignoring Deal Structure (All-Cash vs. Earnout Split)

**Mistake:** "I got offered $10M" (but it's $5M cash + $5M earnout over 3 years, 80% fail rate).

**Reality:** $5M cash today ≠ $5M earnout tomorrow (you'll probably get $1-2M).

**Example:** Deal structured as $6M cash + $4M earnout. You hit targets but buyer makes you an offer: "We'll pay $1.5M earnout now, $0.5M if you stay 2 more years." Most founders settle for $1.5M. **Cost: $2.5M**

**Fix:**
- All-cash is worth 20% premium (take lower number if all cash)
- Earnout should be <20% of total, 12 months max
- Earnout vests regardless of employment
- Model worst-case (assume you get 30% of earnout)

---

## 4. Not Disclosing Issues Proactively

**Mistake:** Data room is clean, but you omit that top customer is leaving (didn't tell buyer). They find out during DD. Trust destroyed.

**Reality:** Buyers assume non-disclosed = you're hiding. Honesty flips the script.

**Example:** You omit that 40% of revenue is from one customer. They discover it, demand 20% price reduction + higher reps cap. **Cost: $500k + higher risk**

**Fix:**
- Disclose issues yourself before DD
- Frame with mitigation plan: "Customer concentration is 40%, but we're diversifying; here's the plan"
- Transparency = buyer trust = better terms

---

## 5. Missing Key Customer Renewals During Deal

**Mistake:** You're in LOI. Top customer is up for renewal. You delay renewal until after close (hoping buyer will close it). Buyer drops the ball. Customer leaves.

**Reality:** Renewals during deal = legal landmines (change of control, assignment risk).

**Example:** $500k customer doesn't renew during DD period. Deal closes at $3M ARR; should have closed at $3.5M. Earnout misses by $500k. **Cost: $500k**

**Fix:**
- Get key customer renewals **before LOI** (soft shop phase)
- Or get written commitment from buyer to close renewals
- Track renewal dates; don't let them fall during LOI/DD
- Build in "no material customer loss" MAC clause

---

## 6. Weak Data Room = Red Flags to Buyer

**Mistake:** Uploading financials with missing months, contracts without signatures, cap table with blank rows.

**Reality:** Incomplete data room screams "hiding something" even if it's just disorganization.

**Example:** Missing Q2 financial statements. Buyer assumes you lost customers. Demands 15% price reduction to account for hidden liabilities. **Cost: $300k**

**Fix:**
- Organize data room to TIER 1 (all critical items) before showing
- Test with friendly investor first
- Have M&A counsel review data room before sharing
- Include "data room updated [DATE]" stamp (shows you maintain it)

---

## 7. Negotiating Reps/Warranties Without Limits

**Mistake:** Buyer asks "Do you have any undisclosed liabilities?" You say "No" without carving out known items.

**Reality:** Reps can be used to claw back money post-close for any breach.

**Example:** You rep "no litigation." Months later, old customer sues. Buyer claws back $200k from escrow. **Cost: $200k+**

**Fix:**
- Carve out known issues upfront (e.g., "except [known pending issue]")
- Cap reps at 0.5-1% of purchase price (not unlimited)
- Survive period <18 months (don't rep forever)
- Get reps insurance ($25-50k) to cover unknown issues

---

## 8. Accepting Overly Broad Non-Compete

**Mistake:** Buyer: "You can't work in SaaS for 2 years globally." You agree.

**Reality:** Post-acquisition, you're bored or buyer deprioritizes your product. You can't restart in your expertise area.

**Example:** You agree to 24-month non-compete worldwide. 6 months post-close, you're frustrated. Can't raise next startup because you can't hire anyone in your space or work there. **Cost: Years of lost opportunity**

**Fix:**
- Non-compete: 6-12 months, not 2 years
- Scope: "In [specific product area]" not "all software"
- Geography: "[Local market]" not worldwide
- Carve-out: Consulting for non-competitors is allowed

---

## 9. Not Getting Written Terms Before Heavy Negotiation

**Mistake:** You and buyer's CEO agree on terms verbally. Two weeks later, their board/legal changes everything.

**Reality:** Verbal agreements are worthless. Written term sheet first, then SPA.

**Example:** CEO promised $10M, founder agrees. Legal says "LOI says all-stock, earnout 3 years." Founder fights for 3 weeks to get back to cash. **Cost: Time + concessions**

**Fix:**
- Get **term sheet in writing** (LOI) before verbal agreement
- Shared doc (Google Sheets) during call (capture terms real-time)
- Advisor reviews before you agree verbally
- Don't say "yes" until it's in the LOI

---

## 10. Underestimating Tax Impact & Structuring

**Mistake:** $10M deal. You don't consult a tax advisor. Deal is structured as "asset sale" = you pay income tax on gains + ordinary income on earn-out + short-term capital gains. Total tax: ~50%.

**Reality:** Structure (asset vs. equity, cash vs. stock, earnout treatment) can swing tax bill by 20-30%.

**Example:** Same $10M deal, but structured as "stock purchase" = long-term capital gains (20%) + deferral strategies = 10% total tax. **Savings: 40% = $4M after-tax**

**Fix:**
- Hire tax advisor **before LOI**
- Model tax impact of each deal structure
- Negotiate structure favorably (equity > asset for tax)
- Consider deferral strategies (earnout deferral, etc.)
- Get earnout treatment clarified in writing

---

## Bonus: 11. No Walk-Away Number

**Mistake:** Buyer offers $7M. You negotiate for $8M. They come back with $7.2M. You're exhausted. You accept.

**Reality:** You never defined your BATNA (best alternative).

**Example:** Your walk-away was $7.5M. You settled for $7.2M. **Cost: $300k + 6 months of your life**

**Fix:**
- **Before soft shop**, write down BATNA (e.g., "Raise Series B at $X valuation or stay independent")
- Calculate exact $ threshold: e.g., "Walk-away if <$X or earnout >25%"
- Share with advisor; have them enforce it
- Actually be willing to walk (don't bluff)

---

## Summary Checklist

Before you sign ANY deal:

- [ ] Advisor reviewed (M&A counsel + investment banker)
- [ ] Countered first offer (didn't accept immediately)
- [ ] Modeled deal structure (cash vs. earnout impact)
- [ ] Disclosed key issues proactively (no surprises)
- [ ] Data room is clean (no missing docs)
- [ ] Reps/warranties are carved out & capped
- [ ] Non-compete is scoped & time-limited
- [ ] Terms are in writing (not just verbal)
- [ ] Tax impact modeled (consult CPA)
- [ ] Have a walk-away number (and stick to it)
