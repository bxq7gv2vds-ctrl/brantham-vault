# Buyer Decision Tree — Which M&A Path For You?

Use this to decide strategy before talking to advisors. Answer 5 questions, find your path.

---

## Quick Start (5 Questions)

**Q1: What's your primary goal?**
- A) Maximize cash exit → Go to Path 1 (Speed + Highest Price)
- B) Maintain control / long-term vision → Go to Path 2 (Strategic Investor)
- C) Get operational support + capital → Go to Path 3 (PE / Growth Equity)
- D) Diversify capital / passive return → Go to Path 4 (Financial Buyer)

**Q2: What's your timeline?**
- A) I need capital in <6 months → Faster timeline
- B) I'm comfortable 9-12 months → Standard
- C) I can wait 18+ months → More selective

**Q3: Are you ready to grow?**
- A) Yes, want to hire aggressively → Growth mode
- B) Want stable, profitable company → Stable mode
- C) Want to stay independent → IPO mode (skip this exercise)

**Q4: Do you want to stay involved?**
- A) I want a role post-close → Founder stay required
- B) I'm open to leaving → More optionality
- C) I want passive return → Exit completely

**Q5: What's your BATNA (walk-away option)?**
- A) Keep growing independently (good market) → Strong BATNA
- B) Fundraise for next round → Medium BATNA
- C) I'm getting burned out / running out of runway → Weak BATNA

## The 4 Buyer Paths

### PATH 1: Speed + Highest Price (Strategic Buyer)

**Who**: Large competitor or adjacent vertical player.
**Timeline**: 90-150 days
**Price**: 5-8x ARR (can go higher if synergies clear)
**Your role**: Maybe, maybe not (depends on fit)
**Capital structure**: Mostly cash at close, small earnout if any

**This path is good if:**
- ✓ You want maximum cash, fast
- ✓ You're OK with integration into their org
- ✓ Your product complements theirs perfectly
- ✓ Your team size is manageable (not huge hiring needed)

**Red flags for this path:**
- ❌ Product is different from their core (won't prioritize it)
- ❌ Customer overlap = they might kill your brand
- ❌ You want long-term autonomy (they'll integrate you)
- ❌ Churn is high (they'll get cold feet)

**Negotiation playbook:**
- Anchor high (5-8x ARR range)
- Emphasize synergies (cost savings + cross-sell)
- Show defensibility (patents, customer moat, network effects)
- Push for cash at close (low earnout)

**Example**: SaaS analytics platform acquired by data warehouse company (5.2x ARR, 18-month integration)

---

### PATH 2: Strategic Investor (Venture-Backed SaaS Buyer)

**Who**: Growth-stage VC-backed company buying for strategic expansion.
**Timeline**: 60-120 days
**Price**: 5-10x ARR (depends on growth rate)
**Your role**: Usually stay (retention important)
**Capital structure**: 70% cash + 30% earnout (milestone-based)

**This path is good if:**
- ✓ You want growth capital + strategic resources
- ✓ You want to stay CEO or key role
- ✓ Your product adds to their platform
- ✓ You believe in their vision (long-term fit)
- ✓ You can help them raise next round

**Red flags:**
- ❌ Buyer's product is in different vertical (execution risk)
- ❌ Your product is too niche (limited cross-sell)
- ❌ You want to exit completely (they want your team)
- ❌ Buyer's market is crowded (competition pressure)

**Negotiation playbook:**
- Focus on synergies (not just your standalone value)
- Propose earnout tied to growth targets (you can influence)
- Negotiate founder retention package (bonus + clear role)
- Push for product roadmap autonomy (keep your vision)

**Example**: Vertical SaaS acquired by enterprise software platform for market expansion (6.5x ARR, 24-month earnout on cross-sell revenue)

---

### PATH 3: PE / Growth Equity

**Who**: PE firm or growth equity fund buying your company as platform or add-on.
**Timeline**: 120-180 days
**Price**: 4-6x ARR (depends on EBITDA margin, growth rate)
**Your role**: Usually CEO (they need you), or exit with retention bonus
**Capital structure**: 50% cash + 50% earnout (tied to growth / profitability)

**This path is good if:**
- ✓ You want operational support (they know how to scale)
- ✓ You want to stay CEO but have a board + controls
- ✓ Your margins are good (they care about profitability)
- ✓ You're growing 30%+ (they want growth equity)
- ✓ You want optionality to sell again in 3-5 years

**Red flags:**
- ❌ Your unit economics are bad (they'll pressure margins)
- ❌ You want autonomy (they add operational partners)
- ❌ Product is early-stage (they need traction)
- ❌ Team quality issues (they'll want to replace you)

**Negotiation playbook:**
- Show unit economics clearly (CAC payback, LTV:CAC)
- Propose founder equity co-investment (skin in game)
- Negotiate governance (seat on board, decision rights)
- Ask for leeway on growth vs. profitability

**Example**: SaaS platform acquired by PE firm as platform, with 2 subsequent add-on acquisitions (5x base, 30% earnout on EBITDA)

---

### PATH 4: Financial Buyer (Family Office, Insurance Company)

**Who**: Non-strategic buyer wanting dividend-generating asset.
**Timeline**: 150-210 days (conservative, slow)
**Price**: 3-5x ARR (conservative multiples)
**Your role**: Maybe stay as employee or advisor
**Capital structure**: 60% cash + 40% earnout (tied to earnings stability)

**This path is good if:**
- ✓ You want conservative, low-risk valuation
- ✓ Your product is mature and stable (not growth mode)
- ✓ You want passive income (dividend consideration)
- ✓ You've built a great team that can run independently
- ✓ You want to step back (founder burnout)

**Red flags:**
- ❌ Your business is growth-dependent (they want stability)
- ❌ You want operational resources (they're hands-off)
- ❌ Technical debt is high (they'll cut R&D)
- ❌ You're early-stage (no diversified customer base)

**Negotiation playbook:**
- Emphasize predictability (low churn, stable revenue)
- Show path to profitability (not just growth)
- Propose dividend structure (they like recurring cash)
- Be realistic on growth projections (they won't believe hockey stick)

**Example**: Established software company with 90%+ NRR acquired by insurance company for portfolio diversification (3.5x ARR, stable dividend)

## Decision Tree (Visual)

```
START
  |
  ├─ Q1: Goal = Maximize cash? → PATH 1 (Strategic Buyer)
  ├─ Q1: Goal = Growth + Control? → PATH 2 (Strategic Investor)
  ├─ Q1: Goal = Operational help? → PATH 3 (PE/Growth Equity)
  └─ Q1: Goal = Passive return? → PATH 4 (Financial)
  
  ├─ Q2: Timeline <6m? → Consider quicker buyer (1 or 3)
  ├─ Q2: Timeline 18+m? → Can be picky (2 or 3)
  
  ├─ Q3: Want growth mode? → PATH 1 or 2
  ├─ Q3: Want profitability? → PATH 3 or 4
  
  ├─ Q4: Want founder role? → PATH 2 (push) or 3
  ├─ Q4: Want to exit? → PATH 1 or 4
  
  ├─ Q5: Strong BATNA? → Wait for best offer (patient)
  ├─ Q5: Weak BATNA? → Close faster (accept reasonable)
```

## Quick Decision Flowchart (One Page)

**Use this with your co-founders / board:**

```
START HERE → Answer Q1 → Q2 → Q3 → Q4 → Q5

Q1: PRIMARY GOAL?
  Cash fast? → PATH 1 (Strategic)
  Growth + control? → PATH 2 (Investor)
  Operational help? → PATH 3 (PE)
  Passive income? → PATH 4 (Financial)

Q2: TIMELINE PRESSURE?
  <6 months → Speed matters, consider PATH 1
  9-12 months → Normal, flexible
  18+ months → Patient, can be selective

Q3: BUSINESS MODE?
  Growth (30%+ YoY) → PATH 2 (they want scale)
  Profitable (15%+ EBITDA) → PATH 3 (they want earnings)
  Stable (<15% growth, profitable) → PATH 4 (they want income)

Q4: FOUNDER STAY?
  Yes, want role → PATH 2 or PATH 3 (negotiate hard)
  No, want exit → PATH 1 or PATH 4 (cleaner)

Q5: WALK-AWAY OPTION?
  Strong (growing well, could IPO) → Be patient, negotiate hard
  Weak (needing capital, burned out) → Close faster, accept 10-20% less

RESULT: Your optimal buyer profile = PATH [1/2/3/4]
```

## Action Plan: Next Steps

**Once you pick a path:**

1. **Month 1**: Prepare materials (CIM, cap table, data room)
2. **Month 1-2**: Identify 15-20 buyers matching your path
3. **Month 2-3**: Warm introductions (use advisors, bankers)
4. **Month 3-4**: First meetings, gauge interest
5. **Month 4-5**: LOI negotiation
6. **Month 5-8**: Due diligence
7. **Month 8-9**: Closing

**Each path has different acceleration tactics:**
- PATH 1: Emphasize synergies, push for speed
- PATH 2: Show growth roadmap, emphasize founder retention
- PATH 3: Show unit economics, talk about scaling
- PATH 4: Talk about stability, low churn, predictability

## Use Case

**Founder thinking about exit:**
```
"I want max cash but also want to stay involved and see my vision realized."
→ PATH 2 (Strategic Investor) = best fit
→ Seek venture-backed SaaS buying in your space
→ Offer co-founder role, earnout on cross-sell growth
```

**Different founder:**
```
"I'm burned out. I want to cash out and not look back."
→ PATH 1 (Strategic) or PATH 4 (Financial)
→ Emphasize stability, no founder role, clean exit
→ Accept lower price for faster closing
```

**Another founder:**
```
"I want to scale aggressively. Need operational support."
→ PATH 3 (PE) = best fit
→ Highlight growth runway, unit economics
→ Negotiate governance for autonomy
```
