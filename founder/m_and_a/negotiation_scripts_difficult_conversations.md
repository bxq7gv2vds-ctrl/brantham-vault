# NEGOTIATION SCRIPTS — DIFFICULT CONVERSATIONS

**Purpose:** Real-world scripts for tough moments (lowball offer, earnout threats, team conflict)  
**How to use:** Read 2-3 times before the call. Paraphrase, don't memorize.  
**Format:** Scenario → Your opening → Expected pushback → Your response → Next step  

---

## SCENARIO 1: Buyer sends lowball (30%+ below ask)

**Situation:** LOI arrived. Price is "$50M" but you asked for $75M. You're angry.

### The call

**Your opening (stay calm):**
```
"Thanks for the LOI. I appreciate you moving fast. I notice the valuation 
is $50M, but we discussed $70-75M range. That's a significant gap. 
Walk me through how you arrived at $50M?"
```

**Why this works:**
- You're not angry (tone matters)
- You're asking them to justify (makes them explain their math)
- You opened with acknowledgment (good faith)

**Expected pushback:**
> "The market is softening. We did comps analysis and $50M is fair for SaaS with your growth profile."

**Your response (don't accept first answer):**
```
"I respect the comps analysis. Can you walk me through it? Because we 
ran our own against [company 1], [company 2], [company 3] — all at 
8-10x ARR. We're at [your metrics], which suggests $70M+.

Here's what I propose: Let's align on the multiple first (both of us 
use the same comps), then apply it to our actual numbers. If we're 
still apart, we structure the gap as earnout we can both earn into."
```

**Why this works:**
- You didn't accept their number as gospel
- You have comps to back you up
- You pivoted to solution (earnout)

**If they push back again:**
```
"I hear you. But here's my constraint: Our board and investors valued 
us at [X] 6 months ago. A 30% haircut signals something material changed 
about the business or your confidence in us. Either way, we need to 
address that before I can sell this to stakeholders.

What changed on your side? Is it market? Or is there something about 
our business you're concerned about?"
```

**Next step:**
- If they cite market issues → Offer earnout tied to growth (they get upside if market recovers)
- If they cite operational concerns → DD deeper before conceding price

## SCENARIO 2: Buyer demands 50%+ earnout ("You finance the deal")

**Situation:** LOI says 30% earnout, but in negotiations they want 60%.

### The call

**Your opening:**
```
"I want to make sure we're aligned on the deal structure. I'm seeing 
a shift toward 60% earnout, but we discussed 30% at LOI. Help me 
understand what changed on your side."
```

**Expected pushback:**
> "We want to de-risk the acquisition. You need to prove post-close that the growth we expect is real."

**Your response (firm):**
```
"I understand de-risking, but here's the problem: You control post-close 
strategy, budget, and hiring. So 60% earnout means you control whether 
we hit earnout targets. That's not risk-sharing — that's you shifting 
risk to us while keeping control.

Here's what works for both of us:
- 30% earnout (1 year) based on standalone metrics we lock in now
- Earnout is gated on metrics YOU can't manipulate (ARR, churn rate)
- We both have skin in the game

If you don't have confidence we'll hit targets post-close, maybe we 
shouldn't do this deal."
```

**Why this works:**
- You called out the misaligned incentives
- You gave them an alternative (30% is reasonable)
- You signaled walk-away (they blink first)

**If they insist on 50%+:**
```
"OK, let's be honest: If you're asking for 50%+ earnout, you're not 
that confident in this deal. And if you're not confident, neither am I. 

Here's where I land:
- 20% earnout IF you want high certainty
- 30% earnout with standalone metrics IF we both commit to growth
- 50%+ earnout = we pass and explore other buyers

Which one works for you?"
```

**Next step:**
- Most buyers retreat to 30-35% (split the difference)
- If they stick to 50%+ → You walk (better deals exist)

## SCENARIO 3: Earnout dispute — Buyer claims you missed targets

**Situation:** It's month 10 post-close. Buyer says you won't hit Year 1 earnout ($500K). They're talking about paying $0.

### The call (with CFO + your lawyer on line)

**Your opening:**
```
"We got your email on earnout trajectory. We disagree with the numbers. 
Let's walk through Q4 actual vs. your forecast, line by line.

Can you walk me through your latest calculation?"
```

**Expected pushback (from their CFO):**
> "ARR is actually $2.2M, not $2.5M. We removed [customer X] because they're in risk category. And we're counting churn differently now."

**Your response (don't accept new definitions):**
```
"Hold on. The SPA Exhibit A defines ARR as [read exact definition]. 
[Customer X] met the inclusion criteria when we signed in June. You 
can't change the definition post-close.

If there's an issue with that customer's retention NOW, that's a churn 
metric — separate from ARR calculation. Let's stick to SPA definitions 
and re-run the numbers together with those rules."
```

**Why this works:**
- You have the SPA locked in (they can't change definitions)
- You're asking them to use agreed-upon rules
- You're staying calm (not accusatory)

**If they claim arithmetic error:**
```
"Can you send me the raw data and your calculation? I want to 
independently verify. If there's an error on our side, we'll own it. 
If there's one on yours, we should fix it together.

Let's get [company] to audit this (3rd-party accountant). I'm happy 
to split the fee. That way we're all confident in the answer."
```

**Next step:**
- Demand 3rd-party audit (accountant) to resolve disputes
- If they refuse → Escalate to their board (red flag)

## SCENARIO 4: Team wants to leave post-close (key person departure)

**Situation:** Your VP Product says she's leaving 6 months post-close. But her retention bonus is vesting over 3 years. Earnout is tied to her staying.

### The call (with your lawyer first)

**Your opening to the buyer:**
```
"I want to give you a heads-up: [Key person] is likely leaving in 6 
months. I know this impacts earnout assumptions. Let's talk about 
how to handle this."
```

**Expected pushback:**
> "That's a material breach of our retention agreement. Her departure 
> affects earnout — we may reduce it."

**Your response (legal + pragmatic):**
```
"I understand the concern. But here's the reality: You can't force 
people to stay. What we CAN do:

1) Keep the earnout as-is, but adjust targets if her departure materially 
   changes the business (e.g., product roadmap shifts)
2) Accelerate her vesting (she leaves happy, earnout stays intact)
3) Hire a replacement and bonus them for continuity

What's your preference?"
```

**Why this works:**
- You're not defensive (people leave)
- You offered 3 concrete solutions
- You put control back on them

**If they're vindictive:**
```
"I get you're frustrated. But punishing us for normal employee attrition 
isn't how partnerships work. [Person] was a contractor on earnout 
milestones, not the entire business.

If you believe the business will underperform without her, we should 
amend the plan. But the earnout shouldn't be zero because one person 
leaves. That's not fair."
```

**Next step:**
- Negotiate: Earnout adjusts based on actual impact, not person departure
- Build contingency plans (backup product leads, etc.) pre-close

## SCENARIO 5: Buyer wants to change integration plan post-LOI

**Situation:** LOI said "standalone product," but 2 weeks later they send integration plan showing your product gets merged into theirs in 3 months.

### The call (URGENT)

**Your opening:**
```
"I got the integration roadmap. This is different from what we discussed 
at LOI. The plan shows our product merging into yours in Q2. Can we 
talk about why that changed?"
```

**Expected pushback:**
> "Our teams aligned on a faster integration timeline. It makes sense 
> strategically."

**Your response (calm but firm):**
```
"I appreciate the thinking, but that timeline is a big change. Here's 
why this matters:

Our earnout is based on growing standalone ARR. If you merge the product 
in 3 months, we lose ability to hit earnout targets. The finance is 
broken. So either:

1) We adjust earnout targets post-integration (to account for merged metrics)
2) We keep standalone standalone for Year 1, then integrate Year 2
3) We amend the deal terms to reflect new strategy

Which works for you? Because right now, it looks like you're trying to 
reduce our earnout by changing the post-close plan."
```

**Why this works:**
- You identified the real issue (earnout impact)
- You offered 3 options (collaborative tone)
- You called out the manipulation (without being accusatory)

**If they push back:**
```
"Look, I'm happy to integrate. But you can't rewrite the post-close 
strategy after LOI and expect the original earnout to stick. That's 
not how deals work.

Let's bring in your COO and my advisors. We'll align on a 100-day plan 
that we BOTH commit to. Then earnout becomes real."
```

**Next step:**
- Demand written 100-day integration plan (before close)
- Earnout should adjust if plan changes post-close

## MASTER RULES FOR DIFFICULT CONVERSATIONS

| Situation | DO | DON'T |
|---|---|---|
| **Lowball offer** | Ask for justification | Accept at face value |
| **Earnout dispute** | Use SPA as source of truth | Negotiate on the fly |
| **Team departure** | Acknowledge + offer solutions | Get defensive |
| **Integration changes** | Escalate immediately | Accept silently |
| **Valuation challenges** | Bring comps + data | Argue emotionally |

---

## TONE CHECKLIST (Before you call)

- ✅ Calm (not angry, even if you want to be)
- ✅ Curious (ask questions, don't accuse)
- ✅ Data-driven (have numbers, not opinions)
- ✅ Solution-oriented (offer 2-3 options)
- ✅ Willing to walk (you have BATNA in mind)

---

## AFTER THE CALL

1. **Recap in writing** — "Here's what I heard; here's what we discussed"
2. **Escalate if needed** — Copy your lawyer on email
3. **Document for record** — This matters if deal disputes go to arbitration later
4. **Schedule follow-up** — Don't leave open loops
