# PRE-OUTREACH READINESS AUDIT

**Timeline:** 1–2 weeks *before* first buyer outreach  
**Effort:** 15–30 min  
**Success criteria:** 14/15 checkmarks = ready to approach buyers

---

## SECTION 1: FINANCIAL READINESS (4 items)

**These are first things buyers ask for. Get them right.**

- [ ] **Last 12 months P&L** finalized and error-checked (revenue, COGS, OpEx)
  - ✓ Reconciled to bank statements
  - ✓ No pending invoices inflating revenue (only cash-collected or signed contracts count)
  - ✓ One-time expenses flagged and separated from recurring costs

- [ ] **3-year financial summary** (P&L, balance sheet, cash flow) prepared
  - ✓ YoY growth rates calculated (ARR/MRR growth visible)
  - ✓ Burn rate and runway calculated (months of cash remaining at current burn)
  - ✓ Any debt, lines of credit, or convertible notes disclosed (buyer will see it in due diligence)

- [ ] **Normalized EBITDA** worksheet created (even if rough)
  - ✓ List of add-backs (stock, founder salary above market, one-time costs)
  - ✓ Calculated conservative/base/aggressive scenarios
  - ✓ Ready to defend these adjustments in calls

- [ ] **Bank accounts and credit cards** audited for unexpected expenses
  - ✓ Any personal or off-books charges cleaned up
  - ✓ All company expenses properly categorized (no black holes in "other")
  - ✓ Accounting is on QuickBooks, FreshBooks, or similar (not Excel-only chaos)

---

## SECTION 2: LEGAL & IP READINESS (4 items)

**Buyers' legal teams will ask. Delays here = dead deals.**

- [ ] **Company structure validated** (clean cap table ready for lawyers)
  - ✓ Incorporation documents pulled (Articles of Incorporation, bylaws)
  - ✓ All equity (common stock, options, RSUs) accounted for and documented
  - ✓ No hidden founding agreements that change ownership (bring-along rights, anti-dilution, etc.)
  - ✓ Option pool is reasonable (10–20% is normal; >30% is red flag)

- [ ] **IP assigned to company** (not sitting in founder's personal name)
  - ✓ Copyright assignment: Code, design, content assigned to company (not GitHub personal)
  - ✓ Patents: If any, utility or provisional patents assigned to company entity
  - ✓ Trademark: Brand name and logo registered in company name (not founder)
  - ✓ No disputed IP: Zero disputes with former employees on code ownership

- [ ] **Contracts with key vendors, customers, partners reviewed** for change-of-control clauses
  - ✓ Top 5 customers: Do they have termination rights if company is acquired? (Get in front of this)
  - ✓ Vendor agreements: Any that require buyer approval to transfer? (SaaS licenses, hosting, payment processors)
  - ✓ Any non-compete or non-solicitation agreements you signed (as founder, from past roles)?
  - ✓ Employee agreements: All NDAs, IP assignments, non-competes in writing

- [ ] **Tax status clean** (no surprises in due diligence)
  - ✓ All payroll taxes paid (no back taxes owed, not even small amounts)
  - ✓ Sales tax collected and remitted (if applicable to your state)
  - ✓ Corporate taxes filed (previous 3 years of 1120-S or 1120)
  - ✓ No audit flags, no liens against the company

---

## SECTION 3: PRODUCT & MARKET READINESS (3 items)

**Buyers care about: Is the product real? Is the market ready? Can they grow it?**

- [ ] **Product is documented** (roadmap, technical docs, dependencies visible)
  - ✓ Architecture documented (even 1-pager is fine, not zero)
  - ✓ Codebase is not a total mess (no "TODO: refactor everything" comments)
  - ✓ Dependencies listed and up-to-date (no EOL frameworks, security patches applied)
  - ✓ Dev setup works: Buyer's engineers can clone and run locally in <1 hour

- [ ] **Market positioning clear** (you can explain your wedge in 30 seconds)
  - ✓ TAM (total addressable market) size estimated (even if rough)
  - ✓ Competitive advantages listed (not "we're better," but *specific* wins)
  - ✓ Customer acquisition cost (CAC) and lifetime value (LTV) calculated or estimated
  - ✓ NPS or CSAT measured (even if informal: "What would you tell a friend?")

- [ ] **Roadmap visible** to buyer (shows you're thinking ahead, not stuck)
  - ✓ 12-month roadmap exists (high-level, not feature-by-feature)
  - ✓ Key milestones identified (e.g., "Paid tier by Q3," "API by Q2")
  - ✓ No roadmap that promises the moon in 6 months (realistic is more credible)
  - ✓ Understand what buyer might *actually* do with your product (not your roadmap, theirs)

---

## SECTION 4: TEAM & OPERATIONS READINESS (2 items)

**Buyers buy teams. Gaps here tank deals.**

- [ ] **Key people locked in (retention agreements or earnout incentives)**
  - ✓ CTO / lead engineer: Will they stay post-acquisition? (Critical)
  - ✓ VP Sales or customer success lead: Customer relationships matter
  - ✓ Any other irreplaceable person: Founder not the only person who matters
  - ✓ Offer retention bonuses if worried (e.g., $X paid at M1 and M6 post-close)

- [ ] **Operations are documented** (buyer's integration team needs to understand your ops)
  - ✓ Process doc: How do you onboard customers? (not just "they sign up")
  - ✓ Support process: How do you handle customer issues? (SLA, response time)
  - ✓ Release process: How do you deploy code? (CI/CD, testing, rollback procedure)
  - ✓ Hiring/FP&A: How do you forecast headcount and costs? (Buyer will need this)

---

## SECTION 5: RED FLAG AUDIT (2 items)

**Honest assessment. If you know the risk, buyer feels it less.**

- [ ] **You've mapped the biggest buyer objections** and have counter-arguments ready
  - ✓ "Your customer concentration is high (top 3 = 50% of revenue)" → Counter: "But they're [industry leader / long-term contract / low churn]"
  - ✓ "Your churn is X%" → Counter: "But it's [seasonal / top cohort is <5% / we fixed it with Y]"
  - ✓ "Your team is small" → Counter: "But we've proven [acquisition model / product-market fit / team quality]"
  - ✓ "Your tech debt is real" → Counter: "But it's isolated to [specific area], roadmap is clear, no critical blockers"

- [ ] **You know your BATNA** (walk-away price if buyer lowballs)
  - ✓ Calculated from multiples + comps (use `pricing_strategy_initial_ask.md`)
  - ✓ Discussed with co-founders / advisors (aligned on walk-away, not just your gut)
  - ✓ Have alternative options: Can you raise another round? Keep growing? (Credibility check)
  - ✓ Can articulate this to yourself, NOT to buyer (internal anchor only)

---

## SCORING

**Count your checkmarks:**

- **14–15:** ✅ **Ready to approach buyers** — You're prepared. Send that first email.
- **11–13:** ⚠️ **Mostly ready, but fix these gaps** — Spend 2–3 days on missing items, then go.
- **<11:** ❌ **Not ready yet** — Spend 1–2 weeks fixing financials, IP, and legal. Come back.

---

## IF YOU'RE NOT AT 14/15, WHAT TO FIX FIRST?

**Priority order (by impact on deal success):**

1. **Financial docs clean** (Section 1) — Buyers ask for this first email
2. **IP assigned** (Section 2) — Deal-killer if messy
3. **Key people locked in** (Section 4) — Red flag if your CTO could walk
4. **Competitive objections mapped** (Section 5) — Shows sophistication, not naivety
5. **Market positioning clear** (Section 3) — Makes the pitch work
6. **Operations documented** (Section 4) — Can wait, but helps in due diligence
7. **Tax status clean** (Section 2) — Rarely blocks at offer stage, but surfaces in DD
8. **Contract review** (Section 2) — Important but less urgent than above

**If you have 1–2 weeks:** Fix items 1–4, then go. You'll be 90% ready.  
**If you have 1 day:** Fix items 1–3 (financials, IP, team), then send that email.
