# FOUNDER NEGOTIATION SCRIPTS — 15 KEY MOMENTS

**Utilité:** Pas de BS. Real language you'll use with buyers. Practice these 10x before first call.

---

## SCRIPT 1: Initial Soft Shop Outreach

**CONTEXT:** First email to buyer after NDA.

```
Subject: Exploring Strategic Options — [Your Company]

Hi [Buyer CEO/Biz Dev],

[1 sentence about our shared world/industry]

I'm reaching out because we might be a good fit. We've built [Product] for [Customer Type], 
and we're growing [metric: $3M ARR, 40% YoY growth]. 

I'd like to explore whether there's a strategic opportunity for [Buyer] to own this business 
and accelerate growth. We have a few other conversations happening, so timing matters.

Would you have 30 min this week or next to discuss?

Thanks,
[Your name]
```

**Key principles:**
- No price in first email
- Casual, not formal
- Create FOMO ("few conversations") without desperation
- Ask for time, not commitment

---

## SCRIPT 2: First Call with Buyer — Opening 5 Min

**CONTEXT:** You have 30 min. Buyer is deciding whether to engage. Use first 2 min to hook.

```
[After hellos and small talk (2 min)]

"[Buyer], thanks for taking the time. Here's why I called.

We've built [Product] for [Customer Segment]. Over [timeframe], we've grown to $3M ARR 
with 40% growth and strong retention. 

Here's the interesting part: [your unfair advantage or market insight].

That means we have a unique position in the market right now, and we think [Buyer] could 
leverage that to [specific outcome for buyer: enter new market, save $X, acquire Y customers, etc.].

I'm early-stage exploring, but if this resonates, I'd love to continue."

[Pause. Let them respond.]
```

**Key principles:**
- Traction first (numbers: ARR, growth, retention)
- One unfair advantage (not 10)
- Benefit to THEM (not why we're great)
- Pause and listen

## SCRIPT 3: "What are you asking for?"

**CONTEXT:** Buyer asks about price early. Don't anchor yet.

```
BUYER: "What's your price?"

YOU: "Great question. I'm not anchoring yet, but I want to be thoughtful about it.

From my side, I've built this to $3M ARR with [team of X]. I've turned down [funding / acquisition] 
offers to stay independent. So I'm profitable and in good shape.

For me to consider a sale, the deal needs to [hit my financial bar AND align with what happens 
to the team / mission / our customers].

Why don't we explore fit first? If this is a win-win, price follows naturally."

[If they push again:]

"I'm happy to share a price range after we both understand if this makes sense. 
Can we spend 30 min on fit, and revisit pricing next week?"
```

**Key principles:**
- Don't anchor until you understand buyer's constraints
- Signal strength (profitable, other offers, didn't need this)
- Deflect to fit
- Force them to invest time before you quote

---

## SCRIPT 4: "We need exclusivity"

**CONTEXT:** Buyer wants to start DD. They ask for exclusivity (you stop talking to others). Say no first.

```
BUYER: "Before we start due diligence, we need a 60-day exclusivity window. 
We're investing X in this process."

YOU: "I hear you. And I respect that. Here's the challenge:

I'm in early conversations with 3 other buyers. I can't freeze them out without understanding 
whether [Buyer] is serious.

What I CAN do:
- Give you a 3-week period (not 60 days) to submit an LOI
- During that time, I'll deprioritize other buyers
- If you submit a strong LOI, we move to 60-day exclusivity

Does that work? And what would a strong LOI look like for you?"
```

**Key principles:**
- Push back on long exclusivity (60 days is too long early-stage)
- Offer shorter exclusivity + milestone (LOI submission triggers extension)
- Clarify what "serious" looks like
- Force buyer to commit before you stop shopping

---

## SCRIPT 5: "Your valuation is too low"

**CONTEXT:** Buyer's first LOI comes in at $30M, you expected $45M.

```
BUYER: "We propose $30M ($10× ARR). This reflects current revenue and growth profile."

YOU: "I appreciate the offer. Here's my perspective:

$10× is on the low end for [segment]. Recent comps in our space are going for 12-15×:
- [Company A] sold for 14× last year
- [Company B] at 12.5×

Our situation:
- 40% YoY growth (peers are 20-30%)
- NRR 115% (indicates strong retention + expansion)
- TAM of $X billion (clear runway)

A fair range is $42-50M (14-16.5×). I'd propose $47M as a starting point. 
What would get you comfortable with 13-14×?"
```

**Key principles:**
- Don't accept first offer. Ever.
- Lead with comps (specific deals + multiples)
- Show your metrics are better
- Suggest counter-offer, not ultimatum

---

## SCRIPT 6: "I want to stay and run the product"

**CONTEXT:** Buyer wants you gone post-close. Push back.

```
BUYER: "We'd like to integrate the product into our platform. Your CEO would transition."

YOU: "I understand. But here's what I'd propose:

The reason we've grown to $3M ARR is we move fast + deep product-market fit. 
That speed comes from my vision and decision-making.

I'd like to stay as [Product Head / Division Lead] for 2-3 years. That means:
- I own roadmap + hiring (in budget you set)
- I report to [your CTO / President]
- We have OKRs (quarterly check-ins)

This protects your investment — you don't want to risk institutional knowledge loss in month 1.

What would a successful transition look like for you?"
```

**Key principles:**
- Don't accept "founder gone in 90 days"
- Frame it as THEIR risk (not yours)
- Offer structured role + clear reporting
- Ask for their vision first

---

## SCRIPT 7: "But we found a technical issue during DD"

**CONTEXT:** Buyer discovers tech debt (old API, no tests, etc.). Tries to reduce price.

```
BUYER: "Our tech team found significant technical debt — the API is legacy, no unit tests. 
We need to reduce price to $35M to account for remediation costs."

YOU: "I appreciate the technical review. Here's what we know:

1. **That debt is known:** Our team has a roadmap to modernize the API (Q3-Q4). 
   We budgeted $150k and 3 engineer-months.

2. **It's not a blocker:** The API works — we process $X in transactions monthly, 
   99.5% uptime. Refactoring is an engineering project, not a risk.

3. **Price impact is minimal:** $150k is 0.5% of a $30M+ deal. It's not a price-adjustment factor.

Here's what I propose:
- We escrow $200k for post-close API modernization (gives you confidence)
- Price stays at $45M (not $35M)
- My team helps document the work

Fair?"
```

**Key principles:**
- Don't let buyer use "we found X" as leverage
- Distinguish between "risk" (could fail) vs "debt" (effort to fix)
- Offer escrow / transition help (show good faith)
- Push back on price reduction for known issues

## SCRIPT 8: "We want 40% earnout"

**CONTEXT:** Buyer wants majority of deal in earnout (low cash at close). Push back.

```
BUYER: "We propose: $25M cash, $20M earnout over 3 years (hit $5M ARR target)."

YOU: "I hear the structure. Here's my concern:

I can't bet 40% of my proceeds on hitting an ARR target over which I have limited control 
post-close. That's too much risk.

Earnout should be 20-25% MAX, for metrics I can influence:
- Product growth metrics (not buyer's distribution)
- Customer retention (metric we control)
- Not "buyer's profit" or "buyer's acquisition activity"

Here's my counter:
- $42M cash (now)
- $3M earnout (18 months, $3.5M ARR milestone — we're already $3M, this is conservative)

That gives me confidence + protects you with a prove-out period. Deal?"
```

**Key principles:**
- Earnout should be <25% of total
- Metric must be within YOUR control (not buyer's sales team)
- Short window (18 months > 3 years)
- Insist on cash-first, earn-out-second

---

## SCRIPT 9: "Your reps cap is too high"

**CONTEXT:** Buyer wants unlimited reps liability (you warrant everything forever).

```
BUYER: "Reps and warranties are uncapped. We want full indemnity in case of legal issues."

YOU: "Uncapped reps create too much tail risk. Here's what's reasonable:

In M&A, standard is:
- **Reps cap:** 1-2% of deal value (you: $30M deal → $300k-600k max liability)
- **Duration:** 18-24 months post-close
- **Basket:** First $50k of claims is your deductible

My proposal:
- $500k cap (1.5% of $30M)
- 18 months post-close
- $25k basket

Anything beyond that is buyer's problem to solve post-close. I'm not an insurance company."
```

**Key principles:**
- Reps cap <1.5% is market standard
- Capped reps protect you from tail risk (lawsuit 3 years later)
- Insist on time-limit (18-24 months)
- Use "basket" to exclude small claims

## SCRIPT 10: "I need X to stay on"

**CONTEXT:** Negotiations are near-close. You want signing bonus + retention package.

```
[After LOI is ~95% agreed, before final signature]

"Before we sign, I want to align on retention.

I'm committing to stay for 3 years and hit growth targets. In exchange, I need:

1. $1M signing bonus (paid at close, separate from purchase price)
   → Reason: It's recognition of my staying, not part of valuation
   
2. $2M earnout-bonus on top of deal earnout 
   → If we hit $5M ARR + my team stays, I get extra $2M
   
3. Quarterly equity refresh ($500k/year × 3 years)
   → Standard for founders staying post-close
   
4. My team: Any layoffs in first 12 months require my approval
   → I need protection for my people

This is standard M&A retention language. Can we finalize this?"
```

**Key principles:**
- Signing bonus is separate from deal price (non-negotiable)
- Earnout bonus rewards YOU for hitting targets (not buyer's bonus)
- Quarterly equity refresh is standard + tax-efficient
- Team protection prevents buyer from gutting your team post-close

## SCRIPT 11: "What does success look like post-close?"

**CONTEXT:** Due diligence call. Buyer reveals integration plans. Probe for misalignment.

```
YOU: "I want to understand what success looks like for you post-close. 
     Walk me through the 90-day plan."

BUYER: [Outlines plans]

YOU: [Listen, then ask:]
- "Who owns the product roadmap in months 1-3?"
- "If my team suggests we kill feature X and build Y instead, who decides?"
- "How do you measure success for us? Revenue? User growth? Integration?"
- "What's the red line for you? What would cause a concern?"
- "If earnout metrics are on track but product isn't what you hoped, can we adjust?"

[Key insight: This call reveals whether buyer respects you or views you as a vendor.]
```

**Key principles:**
- Listen to buyer's 90-day plan before negotiating roles
- Ask who decides roadmap (you or buyer's CTO?)
- Clarify success metrics (revenue vs. customer growth?)
- Red-line questions: Can you push back if plans are wrong?

## SCRIPT 12: "We want to reduce the escrow"

**CONTEXT:** Buyer wants $5M escrow (16% of deal). Push back.

```
BUYER: "We propose $5M escrow, held 2 years, for breaches of reps."

YOU: "That escrow is too high. Here's what's market:

For a $30M deal:
- Escrow: 5-10% ($1.5-3M)
- Duration: 12-18 months
- Release: Automatic if no claims after [period]

My proposal:
- $1.5M escrow (5% of deal)
- 12 months post-close
- Automatic release after 12 months if no liens

Beyond that, you have insurance (representations & warranty insurance). We can split the premium."
```

**Key principles:**
- Escrow holds YOUR cash post-close (highest priority)
- <10% is market standard
- 12 months is industry average
- After 12 months, automatic release

## SCRIPT 13: "I'm talking to other buyers"

**CONTEXT:** Buyer acts like you're exclusive. Remind them you're not.

```
BUYER: "We're investing heavily in this. Are we the preferred buyer?"

YOU: "I appreciate the investment. Here's my transparency:

I'm in serious conversations with 3 buyers. You're in the top 2, but I haven't made a decision.

What determines my decision:
1. **Valuation:** Fair to my investors + me
2. **Certainty:** Can you close? Do you have board approval?
3. **Post-close:** Will you let me run the business? Will you invest in growth?
4. **Team:** Will you respect our people?

You're strong on all fronts. But I owe it to my team to explore all options.

If you want preference, you need to:
- Sharpen your offer (price or terms)
- Move fast (LOI by [date])
- Commit to [specific milestone]

What can you do?"
```

**Key principles:**
- Don't pretend exclusivity if you don't have it
- Create real FOMO (others are serious, not bluff)
- Force buyer to improve offer to get preferentiality
- Keep levers (if buyer slows down, pivot to buyer 2)

## SCRIPT 14: "That earnout metric isn't in my control"

**CONTEXT:** Buyer wants earnout based on "gross margin improvement" (which buyer controls).

```
BUYER: "Earnout is based on hitting 70% gross margin. We'll integrate your infrastructure, 
which should improve margin."

YOU: "I appreciate the logic. But here's the issue:

If gross margin is 'buyer's infrastructure team improves our architecture,' that's not 
in my control. I can't guarantee it.

Earnout should be tied to metrics I can influence:
- Net revenue retention (I own product + CS)
- Customer acquisition (I own sales + product)
- Churn rate (I own product quality)

I'm happy to do earnout on those. But not on [buyer's actions]."
```

**Key principles:**
- Earnout = metric within YOUR control
- Never accept earnout on "buyer's cost improvements" or "buyer's new distribution"
- If buyer wants gross margin, buyer owns the metric (not you)

---

## SCRIPT 15: "Let's do a founder alignment call with my co-founder"

**CONTEXT:** Buyer thinks they've convinced you. Protect yourself with co-founder veto.

```
BUYER: "Great, we're aligned on the LOI terms. Let's move forward."

YOU: "Before we sign, I need to align with [Co-founder]. He's been a partner in this 
    journey, and any major decision needs both of us.

Let's schedule a call [tomorrow / Friday] with my co-founder, your CEO, and our counsel. 
We'll review the LOI together and sign if aligned.

That OK?"

[This prevents buyer from thinking they "closed" with you. Keeps co-founder in loop. 
 Protects both of you from misalignment.]
```

**Key principles:**
- Never sign LOI without co-founder buy-in
- Force joint alignment call
- Prevents "founder overcommitted us" dynamic

---

## RED FLAGS: When to Walk

**Stop negotiating if buyer says:**
- [ ] "Your earnout metric is our profit margin" (uncontrollable)
- [ ] "Reps are uncapped" (tail risk)
- [ ] "We're cutting your team to 3 people" (integrates away value)
- [ ] "You leave in 90 days" (no founder optionality)
- [ ] "Change of control clause in customer contracts" (can't close deal)
- [ ] "We're going bankrupt / out of funding" (buyer can't close)

**Walk if 2+ of these are true.**
