# Anchoring in M&A: Why Your First Number Matters

## The Science: Why Anchoring Works

**Anchoring bias** = humans use the first number they see as a reference point, then adjust from there.

- Buyer hears "$15M" → adjusts down to "$12M" (20% discount feels like "we won")
- Buyer hears "$8M" → adjusts down to "$6M" (25% discount, still feels same)

**Same final price, but your framing changes the psychology.**

---

## The Founder's Dilemma

| Ask Too High | Ask Too Low |
|---|---|
| ❌ Signals delusion (owner out of touch) | ❌ Leaves $2-5M on table (irreversible) |
| ❌ Buyer walks | ✅ Deal happens faster |
| ✅ Anchors high (room to negotiate) | ✅ Buyer feels good about discount |
| | ❌ Signals weakness / limited options |

---

## The Anchoring Formula

```
Your ask should be:
    [Market Multiple × Your ARR] × 1.2 to 1.3

Example: $2M ARR, 4.5x multiple
    = $9M × 1.25 = $11.25M
```

**Not 2x or 3x:** That's delusion, kills deal.  
**1.2-1.3x:** Signals confidence, leaves negotiation room.

---

## When to Use Anchoring (and When NOT)

### ✅ DO Anchor First When:
- You have **multiple interested buyers** (creates BATNA)
- Your **financials are clean** (can defend any number)
- **Growth is accelerating** (believable upside)
- You **have professional representation** (banker, lawyer)

### ❌ DON'T Anchor First When:
- You're **desperate** (buyer will sense it)
- You have **1 buyer only** (no leverage)
- Your financials are **messy** (contradicts high ask)
- You're **unknown to buyer** (no credibility yet)

## The Anchor Spectrum

### Scenario 1: You Have Power (Multiple LOIs)

```
Week 1-2: Strategic buyer emails, offers LOI.
Week 2-3: PE firm also expresses interest.
Week 3: You release first anchor.

Your move: Use banker to send "we've received interest, 
here's a discussion timeline." Let them bid up.

First ask: $12M (1.3x multiple)
Buyer 1 LOI: $10M
Buyer 2 LOI: $9.5M
Final: You pick one, negotiate from $10M baseline.
```

**Outcome:** Anchoring happens naturally via bidding.

---

### Scenario 2: You Have Data (Clean Financials)

```
You've built $1M ARR, 70% growth, 25% churn, NPS 62.
Strategic buyer asks: "What are you worth?"

Your move: Lead with comps, not with a number.

"Looking at similar deals (Dev tools, 70%+ growth), 
we're seeing 6-8x multiples. That'd put us at $6-8M.
But our NRR is higher than those, so we'd expect upper range."

Buyer hears: "$8M" (anchor)
Buyer asks for: discount
Buyer lands at: $7-7.2M

vs.

If you'd asked: "We're worth $7M"
Buyer hears: "$7M" (anchor)
Buyer asks for: discount
Buyer lands at: $5.5-6M
```

**Outcome:** Same effort, better final price via comps.

---

### Scenario 3: You're Unknown (Cold Outreach)

```
Unknown founder, cold email to strategic buyer.
Buyer: "Interested. What's your ask?"

Your move: DEFLECT.

"Great question. We've built $500k ARR, growing 80% YoY.
Different buyers see different synergies, so we're open to understanding
what makes sense for you. What's your integration approach?"

Buyer now anchors (thinks about synergies), you listen.
Then YOU anchor in response.

Buyer: "We usually pay 3-4x for add-ons like this."
You: "That's our baseline, yes. Given our growth and NRR, 
we'd expect closer to 5-6x. Let's see if the unit economics work."

Anchor planted: "5-6x" (yours), not "3-4x" (theirs).
```

**Outcome:** You don't anchor first, but you correct the frame.

## Tactical Anchoring Moves

### Move 1: The Soft Anchor
```
"Our BATNA is $8M (equity + cash). Strategic value might be higher."
→ Anchors at $8M without saying "we demand $8M"
```

### Move 2: The Range Anchor
```
"Comps suggest $6-8M. We think we deserve upper range."
→ Anchors at $8M via range, not a hard ask
```

### Move 3: The Synergy Anchor
```
"You save $2M in CAC + gain $3M revenue = $5M value.
We're asking $7M, so that's profitable day one."
→ Anchors at $7M via math, not emotion
```

### Move 4: The Time Anchor
```
"We have interest from 2 others. If we move to LOI this week, 
we can do 60-day close. Otherwise, timelines slip."
→ Anchors urgency + willingness (but not price explicitly)
```

## Negotiation After Your Anchor

| Buyer Says | Your Response |
|---|---|
| "That's too high" | "What feels right to you?" (let them anchor back, then adjust) |
| "We usually pay 3x" | "Different buyers see different synergies. What's your integration plan?" (reframe) |
| "Our CEO won't approve that" | "What number would work for you? Then we can see if earnout bridges the gap." (shift to deal structure) |
| "We need more diligence" | "Agreed. Here's the data room. Let's align on price parameters now so we don't waste time." (anchor + move forward) |

---

## Common Anchoring Mistakes

| ❌ Mistake | ✅ Fix |
|---|---|
| Ask first without doing homework | Do comps analysis first, anchor based on that |
| Ask huge number out of nowhere | Use 1.2-1.3x rule, defensible |
| Quote a number, never revisit | Come back: "Based on what we found in DD, here's our revised thinking..." |
| Let buyer anchor, stay silent | Counter-anchor immediately, or deflect & make them justify |
| Anchor during weakness (desperation) | Wait until you have 2+ buyers interested |

---

## Decision Tree: Should You Anchor First?

```
Do I have 2+ buyers interested?
  ├─ YES → Let them bid (natural anchoring)
  └─ NO → Go to next

Do I have clean financials?
  ├─ YES → Use comps + range anchor
  └─ NO → Deflect & listen first

Have they asked for a number?
  ├─ YES → Anchor via comps/range/BATNA
  └─ NO → Wait for them to ask
```
