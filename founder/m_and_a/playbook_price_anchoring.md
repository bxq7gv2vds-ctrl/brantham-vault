# PLAYBOOK: PRICE ANCHORING — HOW TO SET AND DEFEND YOUR PRICE

**The first number mentioned in a negotiation anchors the entire discussion. Learn how to anchor HIGH and defend it.**

---

## PRINCIPLE: THE POWER OF THE FIRST NUMBER

**Psychological anchor effect:** The first number mentioned becomes the reference point for negotiation.

**Example:**
- If YOU say: "We're worth $[100]M", buyer thinks "OK, I'll counter at $[75–85]M"
- If BUYER says: "We'll offer $[50]M", you're now defending UP, not DOWN

**Therefore: You must anchor first and anchor high.**

---

## STEP 1: DETERMINE YOUR VALUATION RANGE

### The 3 Tiers of Valuation

| Tier | Definition | Realistic? | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Stretch** | Best-case scenario; optimal market conditions | 20% chance | $[150]M (if everything perfect) |
| **Fair** | Based on comps, your metrics, market multiples | 60% chance | $[100]M (realistic expectation) |
| **Floor** | Minimum you'll accept; walk-away number | 100% commitment | $[70]M (don't sell below this) |

**Anchoring formula:**
- Your opening ask = **Stretch** (e.g., $150M)
- Counter-offer zone = Stretch to Fair (e.g., $100–150M)
- Walk-away = Floor (e.g., <$70M you decline)

---

## STEP 2: BUILD YOUR VALUATION CASE

### Component 1: Comparable Company Analysis

**Find 5–10 peers that sold in past 24 months. Document:**
- Company name, sale date, acquirer, sale price
- ARR at time of sale, growth rate, margin
- Valuation multiple (price / ARR)

| Company | Sale Price | ARR | Multiple | Notes |
|---------|---|---|---|---|
| Peer A | $[80]M | $[10]M | 8x | Faster growth (60% YoY) |
| Peer B | $[120]M | $[15]M | 8x | Larger customer base |
| Peer C | $[50]M | $[8]M | 6.25x | Slower growth (20% YoY) |
| **Your Company** | **?** | **$[12]M** | **? (7–9x expected)** | 40% growth, strong churn |
| **Your Valuation** | **$84–108M** | | **Midpoint: $96M** | |

**Takeaway:** Comps support $[96]M as fair value; anchor at $[120–130]M as opening ask.

---

### Component 2: Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Analysis

**Conservative 3-year projection:**

```
Year 1: $[12]M ARR → $[14]M (20% growth, let's be conservative)
Year 2: $[14]M → $[17]M (20% growth, path to profitability)
Year 3: $[17]M → $[22]M (30% growth, 25% EBITDA margin)

Terminal Value: $[22]M × 6x multiple (SaaS norm) = $[132]M
Discounted @ 15% WACC:

Year 1 CF: $[2]M (20% EBITDA margin) → PV: $[1.7]M
Year 2 CF: $[4]M (25% EBITDA margin) → PV: $[3.0]M
Year 3 CF: $[5.5]M (25% EBITDA margin) → PV: $[3.6]M
Terminal Value PV: $[132]M / 1.15^3 = $[86.8]M

Total Enterprise Value: $1.7 + $3.0 + $3.6 + $86.8 = $[95.1]M
```

**But this is conservative.** If growth accelerates to 35%, valuation could reach $[120]M+.

---

### Component 3: Strategic Value to Buyer

**Identify 3 reasons why YOUR company is worth a premium to strategic buyer:**

1. **Revenue synergy:** Buyer can cross-sell to their customer base
   - Buyer has 500 customers; if 20% adopt your product at $[50]k/year
   - = $[5]M new ARR in Year 1 → adds $[40–50]M of value to buyer
   
2. **Cost synergy:** Eliminate redundant functions
   - Consolidated sales team (-$[2]M), finance (-$[1]M), ops (-$[500]K)
   - = $[3.5]M annual savings → $[21–28]M NPV to buyer

3. **Competitive moat:** Eliminates competitor, consolidates market
   - Buyer's #2 competitor acquired you
   - Without you: buyer loses market share; market commoditizes
   - = Risk reduction worth 15–20% premium

**Strategic value to buyer: $[40 + 25 + 35]M = $[100]M**
**Your valuation should capture some of this: +$[30–50]M**

**Total justified valuation: $[95 + 40]M = $[135]M**

---

## STEP 3: ANCHOR WITH CONFIDENCE

### When Buyer Asks: "What's Your Price?"

❌ **DON'T say:** "We're flexible" or "What are you thinking?" (puts them in control)

✅ **DO say:**
> "Based on our comparable company analysis [point to 5-peer table], our 40% growth rate, and the strategic synergies with [Buyer name], we see our fair value at **$[130–140]M**. 
>
> We're confident in this range because:
> 1. **Comps:** 8–10x multiples for similar-growth SaaS
> 2. **Synergies:** [Your product] could add $[5]M revenue to [Buyer's] customer base
> 3. **Risk mitigation:** Acquiring us prevents [Competitor] from doing the same
>
> That said, we're open to structure that makes sense for both sides — cash, earnout, whatever gets us to the right value."

**Why this works:**
- You anchor HIGH ($130–140M)
- You back it up with data (comps, DCF, synergies)
- You leave room for negotiation ("open to structure")
- You control the narrative

---

## STEP 4: DEFEND AGAINST LOW-BALL OFFERS

**Buyer comes back:** "That's way too high. We can only offer $[70]M."

### Counter-Technique 1: The Reframe

❌ **DON'T:** "You're crazy. We're definitely worth $[130]M."  
✅ **DO:**

> "I appreciate the opening. Let's make sure we're measuring the same thing. Our $[130]M is based on:
> - **Market comps:** [Show your peer analysis showing 8x multiples]
> - **Our metrics:** 40% growth, 70% margins, unit economics that work
> - **Your synergies:** Our research shows we could add $[X]M revenue to your base
>
> Your $[70]M would imply we're worth 5.8x ARR — that's below market for our growth profile. 
>
> What assumptions are you using to get to $[70]M? Let's reconcile them."

**Why this works:** You're not arguing; you're asking them to justify THEIR number. Forces them to do the work and explain the gap.

---

### Counter-Technique 2: Walk Away Credibly

**If buyer won't move off low-ball:**

> "We appreciate the interest, but $[70]M is $[20–30]M below market. At that price, we'd rather keep growing independently. Our growth trajectory suggests we could be worth $[180–200]M in 3 years, so the opportunity cost is high.
>
> If you'd like to reconvene at a valuation that reflects our metrics and your synergies, we're interested. Otherwise, we'll focus on organic growth or talking to other buyers."

**Why this works:**
- Shows you have alternatives (you do: keep growing, or talk to other buyers)
- Credibly walk away; don't bluff
- Forces buyer to re-evaluate their offer

---

### Counter-Technique 3: Show Alternative Offers

**If you have multiple offers, use them:**

> "We have strong interest from [Buyer B] and [Buyer C], with offers in the $[100–120]M range. We prefer [Buyer A]'s strategic fit, but we need the valuation to be competitive with alternatives. Can you meet us at $[110–115]M?"

**Why this works:**
- FOMO (fear of missing out) motivates buyer to increase bid
- You're not bluffing (you have real alternatives)
- You're being transparent (anchors expectation)

---

## STEP 5: KNOW YOUR WALK-AWAY NUMBER

**Define a hard floor:** Below this number, you walk.

**Example:**
- Fair value: $[100]M
- Floor: $[75]M (150 days of cash, enough to keep operating)
- Walkaway: <$[70]M (don't even negotiate; decline)

**Why you need a floor:**
- Keeps you from getting desperate mid-negotiation
- Prevents bad deals that harm you long-term
- Gives you negotiating power (buyer senses you have options)

---

## TACTICAL CHECKLIST: ANCHORING SEQUENCE

1. **Week 0:** Determine your 3-tier valuation (stretch, fair, floor)
2. **Week 0:** Build comps analysis (5–10 comparable sales)
3. **Week 1:** When buyer asks "What's your price?", deliver prepared answer:
   - Your number ($[130–140]M in example)
   - 3 reasons it's justified (comps, synergies, growth)
   - Openness to creative structure
4. **Week 1–2:** If they low-ball, use counter-techniques:
   - Reframe (ask how they got to their number)
   - Walk away credibly
   - Show alternatives
5. **Week 2–3:** Narrow to "realistic range" ($[100–120]M in example)
6. **Week 3+:** Negotiate final structure (cash, earnout, timing)

---

## RED FLAGS: WHEN YOU'RE LOSING NEGOTIATING POWER

🚩 **You revealed your floor number:** ("We won't go below $[70]M") → buyer anchors there  
🚩 **You said "We're flexible":** → buyer treats valuation as negotiable  
🚩 **You compared to LOWER comps:** ("Peer company sold at 6x") → anchors low  
🚩 **You needed money urgently:** ("We need to close by December") → buyer stalls until deadline  
🚩 **You hired expensive advisors:** (banker, lawyers visible) → signals desperation  

---

## FINAL TACTIC: THE STRUCTURE PLAY

**If buyer won't budge on total value, use STRUCTURE to increase effective valuation:**

| Element | Low Structure | High Structure | Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| Base Cash | $[70]M | $[50]M | $[−20]M |
| Earnout | $0 | $[30]M (realistic) | $[+30]M |
| **Total Value** | **$[70]M** | **$[80]M** | **+14%** |
| **Your Real Value** | $[70]M (80% certain) | $[65]M + $[22.5]M (75% prob) = $[87.5]M | **+25%** |

**Why this works:**
- Buyer gets "lower" headline number ($[80]M vs. $[70]M seems like a win)
- You get higher REAL value (earnout structure in your favor)
- Both sides think they won

## EXAMPLES: ANCHORING IN PRACTICE

### Example 1: You Have Multiple Offers

- Buyer A offers: $[100]M
- Buyer B wants to make an offer: ?

**Your anchoring play:**
> "We appreciate Buyer B's interest. We're considering offers in the $[100–125]M range. If you'd like to be competitive, we'd need to see $[110]M+. Are you in that ballpark?"

**Result:** Buyer B anchors to your $[110]M number, not starting lower.

---

### Example 2: You Have No Other Offers (Yet)

- Buyer A offers: $[60]M
- You have no alternative

**Your anchoring play:**
> "We appreciate the offer. Based on our analysis, we see fair value at $[110–120]M. $[60]M is significantly below market multiples (you're offering 5x ARR vs. 8–10x for comparable companies).
>
> We'd like to work with you because of strategic fit. Can you explain your valuation assumptions? Maybe we're missing something."

**Result:** Forces them to justify low offer. Often, they'll increase to $[80–90]M to stay in the game.
