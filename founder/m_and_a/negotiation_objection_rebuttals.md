# 30 BUYER OBJECTIONS + REBUTTALS

**Utilité** : Script ready-to-use. Mémorisez 3-5 par scénario d'acheteur.

---

## PRICE OBJECTIONS (Common first moves)

### OBJ 1: "Your multiple is 2x market"
**Rebuttal A (Soft):** 
"I hear you on comps. Our growth rate (65% YoY) and churn (5%/m) put us in the 75th percentile of SaaS. That justifies a 12x multiple vs 6x median. Happy to show the analysis."

**Rebuttal B (Firm):**
"We ran comps against [Shopify/Datadog/Notion] at similar stages. They sold at 15x+ ARR. We're asking 10x, which is a discount. If that doesn't work, we can explore earnout to bridge the gap."

**When to use:** Rebuttal A = early soft shop. Rebuttal B = LOI stage, buyer is serious.

---

### OBJ 2: "We can build this in 6 months"
**Rebuttal A (Acknowledge):**
"You could build core features. But you'd also need 18m to acquire the customer base we have, train the team, and avoid the 3 pivots we did. Our cost of capital is much lower than your build cost."

**Rebuttal B (Anchor to cost):**
"To replicate this: $2M dev cost (12 eng × 18m) + $1.5M sales cost to rebuild churn + $500k legal/ops. That's $4M sunk cost + 24m delay. We're asking $8M all-in (price + earnout), which is cheaper, faster, and lower risk."

**When to use:** Rebuttal A = testing if they're serious. Rebuttal B = they're trying to lowball.

---

### OBJ 3: "The market is softening"
**Rebuttal:**
"Understood. That affects FUTURE growth, not the cash we generated THIS year. Our $1.2M ARR is real, growing 65% YoY despite macro. If market softens, we de-risk by getting acquired now at a healthy multiple, rather than waiting 12m and selling at lower growth rate."

**Note:** Don't fight macro. Instead, reframe as urgency FOR THEM (lock growth now, avoid softening).

---

### OBJ 4: "Our internal team can do this cheaper"
**Rebuttal:**
"Maybe. But your team is focused on [X product]. Adding this means context switching, hiring delays, and 2-3 failed attempts (typical for new verticals). We've already mapped the customer journey, built the integrations, and proven unit economics. You buy 5 years of learnings for 1 year of build."

**Subtext:** You're buying optionality, not commoditized labor.

---

### OBJ 5: "Earnout won't work because we'll change your roadmap"
**Rebuttal:**
"Fair point. Let's structure the earnout around outcomes you control: ARR (cash), churn (we help, you implement), retention (our playbook). Not around feature launches (which you'll change). That way, we're aligned on revenue, not micromanagement."

**Key:** Pivot earnout from output-based (features) to outcome-based (cash metrics).

---

## TEAM / CULTURE OBJECTIONS

### OBJ 6: "We need to know your team will stick around"
**Rebuttal:**
"Exactly. Let's structure a retention package: 12m golden handcuffs (equity, earnout linkage) + clear post-close roles for each founder/key hire. If someone leaves, earnout adjusts downward. Aligned incentives keep people."

**Action:** Offer to negotiate founder employment agreement (title, comp, vesting cliff).

---

### OBJ 7: "Your founding team is too [young/old/specialized]"
**Rebuttal A (If junior):**
"Our strength is execution, not just ideas. We've built a $1.2M ARR business and a team of 8 that runs itself. Your team can mentor and we accelerate your roadmap. Fresh perspective + your operational experience = 2+2=5."

**Rebuttal B (If senior):**
"We're at the stage where we need to scale beyond founder leadership. We've hired a COO (name + background). We're ready to move into your operating structure."

---

### OBJ 8: "Key person risk — the CEO is too critical"
**Rebuttal:**
"Valid concern. We've documented all processes, trained the COO/CTO on major customer relationships, and built a bench. The CEO can step into [specific advisory role] post-close. Let's do a 30-day transition plan to derisk this."

**Action:** Create org chart showing founder's role post-close (not essential).

---

## TRACTION / PRODUCT OBJECTIONS

### OBJ 9: "Your churn is higher than industry average"
**Rebuttal A (If true):**
"Our churn is [5%/m] vs [4% for Tier-1]. But we've identified the cohort (SMB, <$50k spend) and improved retention 2% in last quarter via [product/pricing]. By next year, churn hits 3%. That's factored into our forecast."

**Rebuttal B (If it's a bucket issue):**
"That depends on how you measure. MRR churn is [3%]. Logo churn is [8%] because SMBs churn more. We've segmented the forecast by cohort, and the LTV:CAC in the core segment is [4:1]. That's the unit we should anchor valuation on."

**Key:** Normalize churn for cohort (don't compare apples to oranges).

---

### OBJ 10: "Your product is too narrow/vertical"
**Rebuttal:**
"It's vertical-focused BY DESIGN. Verticals have higher NPS, better churn, and sustainable moats. We could generalize, but we'd compete with [Salesforce/HubSpot]. Instead, we own [hair salons/tax software/insurance brokers]. Your platform can tuck this vertical in without distraction."

**Subtext:** Vertical focus = defensible, not a weakness.

---

### OBJ 11: "Your tech stack is outdated"
**Rebuttal:**
"Our stack [Python/Django/React] is mature and boring — exactly what enterprises want. Zero security risk, tons of libraries, easy to hire for. If you want to refactor to [Rust/Go], you can, post-close. Today, it's [stable/performant/maintainable] and gets the job done."

**Key:** Differentiate between "boring is safe" vs "actually broken."

---

## DUE DILIGENCE / RISK OBJECTIONS

### OBJ 12: "We found a contract that needs customer consent to assign"
**Rebuttal:**
"Good catch. Let's classify contracts by: (A) auto-assign no consent, (B) need verbal permission, (C) need written amendment. For (B) and (C), we'll reach out to the customer in week 1 post-LOI. Our customer relationships are strong (NPS 72), so consent is 95%+ likely."

**Action:** Have customer assignment list ready with consent risk per contract.

---

### OBJ 13: "Your IP might be exposed — did you use GPL code?"
**Rebuttal:**
"We've audited dependencies via [FOSSA/Black Duck]. [99%] of code is MIT/Apache/BSD licensed. [GPL code usage]: none (or: used in [isolated component] behind our API). We've confirmed with our engineering team that no GPL is leaked. Happy to provide the full audit."

**Action:** Run code audit BEFORE M&A. Don't let this surprise you.

---

### OBJ 14: "You have a customer concentration risk — top 3 = 45% revenue"
**Rebuttal:**
"Acknowledged. But context: (1) those 3 customers have multi-year contracts + expansion roadmap, (2) we're signing 2-3 new $10k/m customers per quarter (reducing %). (3) if any of the 3 churn, we have triggers to escalate. By end of next year, top 3 = 30%."

**Action:** Show net revenue retention per customer tier. Diversification roadmap.

---

### OBJ 15: "One founder is non-compete + could start a competitor"
**Rebuttal:**
"Fair risk. Let's address in the employment agreement: 3-year non-compete, $X holdback, and [equity cliff/clawback] if they trigger it. This aligns their incentive to grow the business, not launch a copycat."

---

## EARNOUT OBJECTIONS

### OBJ 16: "Earnout metrics are too optimistic"
**Rebuttal:**
"Let's reality-test them. Our 3-year forecast assumes [65%] YoY growth. Over the last 2 years, we've actually grown [68%] YoY. So the earnout targets are slightly conservative. Happy to adjust if you model a different CAC/LTV, but our base case is achievable."

**Key:** Anchor earnout to YOUR 3-year track record, not new assumptions.

---

### OBJ 17: "Earnout will fail because [sales team leaves / product roadmap changes]"
**Rebuttal:**
"Valid. Let's structure earnout around metrics YOU control: ARR (cash generated), not features (your call). If sales team leaves, earnout adjusts downward — that's shared risk. If you kill the product, earnout resets to zero. We're aligned on not sabotaging the business."

---

### OBJ 18: "Earnout is structured as debt — our CFO won't approve it"
**Rebuttal:**
"Understood. Let's restructure as a seller note vs earnout: you pay me [X] today, [Y] over 24m tied to ARR hitting $2M. From an accounting perspective, that's equity/deferred comp, not debt. Your CFO can model both and we'll adjust."

**Key:** Earnout is an accounting classification issue. Reframe if needed.

---

## ESCROW / WARRANTIES OBJECTIONS

### OBJ 19: "We need 2% reps & warranties escrow, 4-year tail"
**Rebuttal:**
"That's 2x market for a software company. Standard is 0.5-1% for 18m. I'll offer: 1% escrow for 18m, with [tax reps] extending to 3 years (if applicable). Tail insurance covers the rest. That protects you, protects us, and is market-rate."

**Action:** Know market reps for your category. Don't accept >1% without push-back.

---

### OBJ 20: "Our lawyers want to carve out [customer churn / product bugs / employee misclassification]"
**Rebuttal:**
"Those are ALL known issues we've disclosed. Customer churn is industry-normal (5%/m). Product bugs are in every software company — we maintain SLA. Employee misclassification — all are W-2 employees per [state law]. Since these are known/disclosed, they shouldn't be carved out of reps. If they discovered NEW facts (bugs we hid), then yes, we're liable."

**Key:** Disclosed = no warranty. Unknown = warranty.

---

## STRATEGIC OBJECTIONS

### OBJ 21: "This deal doesn't align with our 5-year strategy"
**Rebuttal:**
"I hear you. Let's map it: your strategy is [X]. This acquisition [directly enables / complements / speeds up] X by [Y reason]. Here's how it fits: [product roadmap alignment]. If there's a disconnect, let's explore a different structure (minority stake, licensing, partnership) that DOES fit your strategy."

**Key:** Don't defend the deal as-is. Reframe to fit THEIR strategy.

---

### OBJ 22: "We'd rather acquire a larger competitor with [5M ARR, 20% slower growth]"
**Rebuttal:**
"You could. But compare: (1) their growth is slowing (20% → consolidation risk), (2) their team is larger (more integration cost), (3) our NPS is [72] vs theirs [55] (lower churn, higher retention). We're the growth story at lower risk. Plus, price per dollar of ARR is lower (we're cheaper per unit of growth)."

**Key:** Sell differentiation on growth + unit economics, not size.

---

## TIMING / SEASON OBJECTIONS

### OBJ 23: "We need to wait for [budget cycle / board approval / funding round]"
**Rebuttal:**
"Understood. What's the timeline? If [Q4 board approval], we can run LOI negotiations in the meantime, finalize key terms, and speed close once approved. That actually HELPS your timeline — you're ready to sign 60 days after approval, not 90. Happy to pause formal process but keep momentum."

**Key:** Don't drop deal. Keep low-pressure momentum alive.

---

## PRICE / STRUCTURE OBJECTIONS

### OBJ 24: "Can you take [20% / 30%] in equity rollover instead of cash?"
**Rebuttal A (If good acquirer):**
"I'd consider [10-15%] equity rollover if: (1) it's in voting shares, (2) I keep board seat or observer rights, (3) there's a 4-year ramp (not cliff). That way, I'm aligned if you 5x the business. But I need [X cash] for personal reasons, so the base offer needs to be [Y]."

**Rebuttal B (If risky acquirer):**
"I appreciate the offer, but I need cash for personal reasons [family / taxes / life]. Equity upside is great if the business works — but if it doesn't, I need to have exited. Let's structure the earnout to capture upside instead."

**Key:** Equity rollover = riskier. Only do it if buyer is strong + you trust them.

---

### OBJ 25: "Can you seller-finance [20% / 30%] to help close the deal?"
**Rebuttal:**
"I'd be open to seller financing [10%] if: (1) the interest rate is [SOFR + 2%], (2) there's a [2-year repayment], (3) security is [first lien on assets or equity]. But I need [X cash] today for personal reasons. If you can't get the full acquisition price in cash, maybe we should explore a different structure (minority stake, earn-in, partnership)."

**Key:** Seller financing = you become their creditor (risky). Only do it strategically.

---

## WALKAWAY TRIGGERS

### OBJ 26: "We're dropping out of the process"
**Rebuttal A (If bad buyer):**
"Understood. Good luck. If circumstances change, my door is open — but only at [walk-away price] or above."

**Rebuttal B (If good buyer, unexpected drop):**
"I'm surprised — we seemed aligned. Can I understand the reason? If it's [X concern], maybe we can solve it. If it's financial/strategic, I respect the decision. Can we keep a line open for future conversations?"

**Key:** Don't beg. Keep dignity. Future buyers talk to past buyers.

---

### OBJ 27: "Your price expectation is too high for us"
**Rebuttal:**
"I understand. Your ROIC requirements and our growth stage might not align. Let me ask: at what price WOULD this fit your investment thesis? If there's a number that works for both of us, let's explore it. If not, maybe we're not the right fit right now."

**Key:** Flush out their BATNA. Maybe you're misaligned on value, not price.

---

## CLOSING OBJECTIONS

### OBJ 28: "We need to close by [date], or the deal is off"
**Rebuttal:**
"I understand budget cycles. We're committed to hitting [date] if we stay on track with DD questions. Can we agree on a 2-week buffer if an issue surfaces? For example, if we discover [X] in week 7 of DD, we solve it, not restart."

**Key:** Get specific on what CAUSES slippage. Build in contingencies.

---

### OBJ 29: "Our board rejected the deal. We're walking away."
**Rebuttal:**
"That's disappointing. Can I ask: was it [price / strategic fit / risk]? If it's fixable, I'd like to know. If not, I respect the decision. If your situation changes — new board priorities, new funding — let me know."

**Key:** Don't burn bridge. Future CEO might overrule past board.

---

### OBJ 30: "We want to use [offshore engineering team] post-close to save cost"
**Rebuttal:**
"Understood cost optimization. But our customers expect us to [maintain SLA / support in-timezone / keep team continuity]. If you change those, revenue drops. Let's model cost-benefit: how much you save in ops vs churn risk. Maybe we keep core team + add offshore for [non-customer-facing work]."

**Key:** Defend quality. Make cost-cuts founder-proof.

---

## USAGE

**Print 5-10 of these.** Memorize 1-2 rebuttals per objection. In real conversations:
1. Listen fully (don't interrupt)
2. Acknowledge their concern
3. Pivot to your rebuttal
4. Ask clarifying question back

**Goal:** Not "win" the argument, but keep conversation moving + prove you're reasonable.
